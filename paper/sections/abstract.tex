\begin{abstract}
Energy efficiency is a paramount concern in Internet of Things (IoT) sensor networks deployed for continuous air quality monitoring. The high sampling rates, multi-channel data acquisition, and persistent wireless transmission required for reliable pollutant tracking impose substantial energy demands on battery-powered and mains-powered edge devices. This paper proposes a Variance-Aware Adaptive Bit-Width Quantisation (VABQ) framework that jointly optimises sensor-level analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) resolution and inference-level post-training quantisation (PTQ) to minimise total system energy while preserving monitoring fidelity. The framework exploits the inherent temporal structure of indoor air quality signals---long stable periods interspersed with activity-driven transients---to dynamically allocate bit-widths of 4, 8, or 16 bits at the sensor stage. It then applies INT8 PTQ to a lightweight one-dimensional convolutional neural network (1D-CNN) for pollutant event classification at the inference stage. Experiments are conducted on the DALTON indoor air quality dataset, which spans 30 deployment sites over six months using custom ESP32-based sensor nodes measuring PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$, CO$_2$, VOC, CO, NO$_2$, temperature, and relative humidity at 1~Hz. Results demonstrate that VABQ achieves a 61.3\% reduction in sensor-level ADC energy and a 74.8\% reduction in inference energy relative to full-precision baselines, while maintaining pollutant classification accuracy above 96.1\% and normalised root-mean-square error below 2.4\%. A comparative study against uniform static quantisation, fixed-threshold adaptive sampling, SAX-LZW compression, and inference-only PTQ confirms the superiority of the proposed dual-layer scheme. The principal novelty lies in the unified optimisation of sensing and inference quantisation through a variance-entropy criterion, bridging two traditionally separate compression domains within a single pipeline.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} sensor quantisation; energy efficiency; air quality monitoring; adaptive bit-width; post-training quantisation; edge AI; Internet of Things; DALTON dataset
