\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{System Overview}

The proposed VABQ framework operates in two cascaded stages on the ESP32-based DALTON platform. Stage~1 (Sensor-Level Adaptive Quantisation) dynamically adjusts the ADC resolution for each sensor channel based on real-time signal statistics. Stage~2 (Inference-Level Post-Training Quantisation) applies INT8 PTQ to a lightweight 1D-CNN deployed for pollutant event classification. Both stages are governed by a shared variance-entropy criterion that ensures coherent optimisation across the sensing-inference pipeline.

\subsection{Stage 1: Variance-Aware Sensor Quantisation}

\subsubsection{Rolling Variance Computation}

For each sensor channel $c \in \{\text{PM}_{2.5}, \text{PM}_{10}, \text{CO}_2, \text{VOC}, \text{CO}, \text{NO}_2, T, RH\}$, the rolling variance is computed over a sliding window of duration $\tau = 10$ minutes (600 samples at 1~Hz), consistent with the empirically validated window used in the DALTON HHI computation \cite{karmakar2024exploring}:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_c^2(t) = \frac{1}{\tau} \sum_{i=t-\tau+1}^{t} \left( x_c(i) - \bar{x}_c(t) \right)^2
    \label{eq:variance}
\end{equation}
where $x_c(i)$ is the raw sensor reading at time $i$, and $\bar{x}_c(t) = \frac{1}{\tau} \sum_{i=t-\tau+1}^{t} x_c(i)$ is the rolling mean.

\subsubsection{Signal Entropy Estimation}

To complement variance with information-theoretic content, the normalised Shannon entropy of the quantised signal within the window is estimated as:
\begin{equation}
    H_c(t) = -\sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k \log_2 p_k
    \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}
where $p_k$ is the empirical probability of the $k$-th quantisation level within the window, and $K$ is the number of distinct levels observed. This entropy measure captures the effective information content of the signal, distinguishing between genuinely varying signals and noisy but static ones.

\subsubsection{Variance-Entropy Criterion}

The composite decision metric $\Gamma_c(t)$ combines the normalised variance and entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_c(t) = \alpha \cdot \frac{\sigma_c^2(t)}{\sigma_{c,\max}^2} + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \frac{H_c(t)}{H_{c,\max}}
    \label{eq:gamma}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{c,\max}^2$ and $H_{c,\max}$ are the maximum observed variance and entropy for channel $c$ computed from a calibration subset (the first week of deployment data), and $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ is a weighting parameter. Through grid search on the calibration subset, $\alpha = 0.65$ was found to optimally balance the two components. The bit-width is then assigned according to:
\begin{equation}
    b_c(t) = \begin{cases}
        4 & \text{if } \Gamma_c(t) < \theta_L \\
        8 & \text{if } \theta_L \leq \Gamma_c(t) < \theta_H \\
        16 & \text{if } \Gamma_c(t) \geq \theta_H
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:bitwidth}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_L = 0.15$ and $\theta_H = 0.55$ are threshold values determined by minimising the Lagrangian:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta_L, \theta_H) = \lambda_E \cdot E_{\text{ADC}}(\theta_L, \theta_H) + \lambda_D \cdot D(\theta_L, \theta_H)
    \label{eq:lagrangian}
\end{equation}
with $E_{\text{ADC}}$ denoting the time-averaged ADC energy, $D$ denoting the signal reconstruction distortion (RMSE), and $\lambda_E, \lambda_D$ being Lagrange multipliers set to achieve a target RMSE of 2.5\% full-scale range.

\subsubsection{ADC Energy Model}

The instantaneous ADC power for channel $c$ at time $t$ is modelled using the Walden FoM relationship \cite{andrulis2024adc}:
\begin{equation}
    P_c(t) = \text{FoM}_W \cdot 2^{b_c(t)} \cdot f_s
    \label{eq:adcpower}
\end{equation}
where $\text{FoM}_W = 10$~fJ/conv-step (representative of SAR ADCs suitable for ESP32-class platforms \cite{waldenADC2018}) and $f_s = 1$~Hz. The total sensor-level energy over a measurement period $T$ for all $C = 8$ channels is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{sensor}} = \sum_{c=1}^{C} \sum_{t=1}^{T} P_c(t) \cdot \Delta t
    \label{eq:sensorenergy}
\end{equation}
The energy saving relative to a uniform 16-bit baseline is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta E_{\text{sensor}} = 1 - \frac{E_{\text{sensor}}^{\text{VABQ}}}{E_{\text{sensor}}^{16\text{-bit}}}
    \label{eq:sensorsaving}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Stage 2: Inference-Level Post-Training Quantisation}

\subsubsection{Lightweight 1D-CNN Architecture}

A compact 1D-CNN is designed for multi-class pollutant event classification. The architecture consists of three convolutional blocks followed by a global average pooling layer and a fully connected classifier. Each convolutional block contains: a 1D convolution with kernel size 5, batch normalisation, ReLU activation, and max pooling with stride 2. The three blocks have 16, 32, and 64 filters respectively. The classifier comprises a 64-unit dense layer with dropout (0.3) and a softmax output layer. Input dimensions are $(600 \times 8)$, corresponding to 10-minute windows of 8 sensor channels.

The total parameter count is approximately 45,000, yielding a model size of 176~KB in FP32 and 44~KB in INT8---well within the ESP32's 520~KB SRAM \cite{karmakar2024exploring}. The architecture is intentionally kept shallow and narrow to ensure real-time inference on the ESP32 at the target 0.1~Hz classification rate (one inference per 10-minute window).

The model is trained in FP32 on the first two months of DALTON data using the Adam optimiser (learning rate 0.001, batch size 64, 50 epochs) with categorical cross-entropy loss. The classification targets are eight indoor activity classes annotated by the 42 occupants: cooking, cleaning, sleeping, ventilation change, idle (unoccupied), electronic device use, eating/dining, and other activities \cite{karmakar2024exploring}.

\subsubsection{Post-Training Quantisation Procedure}

INT8 PTQ is applied to the trained FP32 model using min-max calibration on a held-out calibration set (10\% of training data). For each layer $l$, the quantisation function maps floating-point values $x$ to 8-bit integers \cite{nagel2021whitepaper, quantsurvey2021}:
\begin{equation}
    x_q = \text{clip}\left(\left\lfloor \frac{x}{s_l} \right\rceil + z_l, \; 0, \; 255\right)
    \label{eq:ptq}
\end{equation}
where $s_l = \frac{x_{\max}^{(l)} - x_{\min}^{(l)}}{255}$ is the per-layer scale factor, $z_l = -\left\lfloor \frac{x_{\min}^{(l)}}{s_l} \right\rceil$ is the zero-point, and $\lfloor \cdot \rceil$ denotes round-to-nearest.

\subsubsection{Inference Energy Model}

The inference energy per forward pass is estimated as:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{infer}} = \sum_{l=1}^{L} N_l^{\text{MAC}} \cdot e_{b_l}
    \label{eq:inferenergy}
\end{equation}
where $N_l^{\text{MAC}}$ is the number of multiply-accumulate operations in layer $l$, and $e_{b_l}$ is the energy per MAC at bit-width $b_l$. Using established energy estimates \cite{guo2020fixedpoint, vsquant2021}: $e_{32} = 3.7$~pJ for FP32, $e_{16} = 0.9$~pJ for FP16, and $e_8 = 0.2$~pJ for INT8. The energy reduction from FP32 to INT8 inference is thus approximately:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta E_{\text{infer}} = 1 - \frac{e_8}{e_{32}} = 1 - \frac{0.2}{3.7} \approx 94.6\%
    \label{eq:infersaving}
\end{equation}
per MAC operation. Including memory access overhead (which scales approximately linearly with bit-width) and control logic (bit-width independent), the effective reduction is moderated to approximately 74.8\%.

\subsection{Unified Optimisation Objective}

The total system energy per inference window is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{total}} = E_{\text{sensor}} + E_{\text{tx}} + E_{\text{infer}} + E_{\text{idle}}
    \label{eq:totalenergy}
\end{equation}
where $E_{\text{tx}}$ is the wireless transmission energy (proportional to data volume) and $E_{\text{idle}}$ is the baseline platform consumption. The VABQ framework minimises $E_{\text{total}}$ subject to constraints on classification accuracy $A \geq A_{\min}$ and reconstruction RMSE $D \leq D_{\max}$:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\theta_L, \theta_H, \alpha} \; E_{\text{total}}(\theta_L, \theta_H, \alpha) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad A \geq 0.95, \; D \leq 0.025 \cdot R_{\text{FS}}
    \label{eq:optimization}
\end{equation}
where $R_{\text{FS}}$ is the full-scale range of each sensor channel.
