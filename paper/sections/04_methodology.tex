\section{Proposed Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section presents the proposed hybrid scheme for energy-efficient air quality monitoring, integrating sensor-side dynamic quantization with hardware-aware temporal convolutional networks. The methodology comprises four interconnected components: (i) dynamic sensor-side quantization with adaptive range scaling, (ii) temporal subsampling and residual encoding, (iii) TCN architecture design optimized for quantized inputs, and (iv) hardware-aware training and deployment protocols. Figure~\ref{fig:system_overview} illustrates the end-to-end pipeline from raw sensor measurements to edge-based predictions.

\subsection{System Architecture Overview}

The proposed system operates in a hierarchical edge-cloud architecture consisting of three tiers. At the \textit{sensor tier}, DALTON nodes sample six pollutant channels (PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$, CO$_2$, NO$_2$, VOC, CO) at 1~Hz and apply dynamic quantization and temporal subsampling locally. Quantized packets are transmitted wirelessly to an \textit{edge gateway} (e.g., Raspberry Pi 4) every $T_S = 4$ seconds. The edge gateway maintains sliding windows of quantized observations and performs inference using a quantized TCN model to generate 10-minute ahead PM$_{2.5}$ forecasts. Predictions and raw data summaries are optionally forwarded to a \textit{cloud backend} for long-term storage, model retraining, and visualization. This architecture minimizes sensor-to-gateway transmission energy while enabling real-time, privacy-preserving predictions at the edge \cite{himeur2024edge, fici2025sensors}.

\subsection{Dynamic Sensor-Side Quantization}

\subsubsection{Uniform Mid-Rise Quantizer}

Each pollutant channel $c \in \{1, 2, \ldots, 6\}$ is quantized using a uniform mid-rise quantizer with $b_c$ bits, dynamic range $[a_c(t), b_c(t)]$, and step size
\begin{equation}
\Delta_c(t) = \frac{b_c(t) - a_c(t)}{2^{b_c}}.
\label{eq:step_size}
\end{equation}
For a raw sensor measurement $x_t^{(c)} \in \mathbb{R}$ at time $t$, the quantized value is
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x}_t^{(c)} = Q_c(x_t^{(c)}; a_c(t), b_c(t), b_c) = \text{clip}\left( a_c(t) + \Delta_c(t) \cdot \left\lfloor \frac{x_t^{(c)} - a_c(t)}{\Delta_c(t)} + \frac{1}{2} \right\rfloor, a_c(t), b_c(t) \right),
\label{eq:quantization}
\end{equation}
where $\text{clip}(\cdot, a, b)$ constrains the output to $[a, b]$ and $\lfloor \cdot \rfloor$ denotes the floor function. The quantization index $k_t^{(c)} \in \{0, 1, \ldots, 2^{b_c}-1\}$ transmitted over the wireless link is
\begin{equation}
k_t^{(c)} = \left\lfloor \frac{x_t^{(c)} - a_c(t)}{\Delta_c(t)} + \frac{1}{2} \right\rfloor.
\label{eq:quantization_index}
\end{equation}
At the receiver (edge gateway), the quantized value is reconstructed as
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_t^{(c)} = a_c(t) + k_t^{(c)} \cdot \Delta_c(t).
\label{eq:reconstruction}
\end{equation}

The quantization noise introduced by this uniform quantizer, assuming input uniformly distributed within each quantization bin, has variance \cite{gray1998quantization}
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{q,c}^2 = \frac{\Delta_c^2(t)}{12}.
\label{eq:quantization_noise}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Adaptive Range Scaling}

Static quantization ranges defined over the entire DALTON dataset lead to inefficient bit allocation, as pollutant concentrations vary significantly across sites, seasons, and activities \cite{karmakar2024exploring}. To exploit this variability, we implement \textit{adaptive range scaling} that updates $[a_c(t), b_c(t)]$ periodically based on recent observations. Every $T_R = 300$ seconds (5 minutes), each channel's range is recalibrated using the empirical 1st and 99th percentiles of the last $N_R = 300$ samples:
\begin{equation}
a_c(t) = P_1\left( \{x_{t-N_R+1}^{(c)}, \ldots, x_t^{(c)}\} \right), \quad
b_c(t) = P_{99}\left( \{x_{t-N_R+1}^{(c)}, \ldots, x_t^{(c)}\} \right),
\label{eq:adaptive_range}
\end{equation}
where $P_\alpha(\cdot)$ denotes the $\alpha$-th percentile operator. This percentile-based approach is robust to transient outliers (e.g., sensor glitches) while capturing the dominant operating range \cite{karmakar2024indoor}. The updated ranges $\{a_c(t), b_c(t)\}$ are transmitted as metadata alongside quantized indices every $T_R$ seconds, incurring negligible overhead (24 bytes per update for 6 channels with 32-bit float ranges, compared to 18-72 bytes per quantized sample depending on $b_c$).

Adaptive scaling reduces effective quantization noise by tightening $\Delta_c(t)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:step_size}, particularly during stable periods (e.g., overnight when pollutant levels are low and range-constrained). As shown in Section~\ref{sec:results}, this mechanism improves RMSE by 0.2-0.3 $\mu$g/m$^3$ compared to static ranges without increasing transmission energy.

\subsubsection{Temporal Subsampling and Residual Encoding}

To further reduce transmission frequency, we exploit the high temporal autocorrelation of indoor pollutants \cite{karmakar2024exploring}. Instead of transmitting every 1~Hz sample, the sensor node transmits quantized packets every $T_S = 4$ seconds. Within this interval, samples are aggregated using one of two strategies:

\paragraph{Absolute Quantization (AQ)}
Transmit the quantized value of the last sample in each $T_S$-second window:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x}_{kT_S}^{(c)} = Q_c(x_{kT_S}^{(c)}), \quad k \in \mathbb{N}.
\label{eq:absolute_quantization}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Residual Quantization (RQ)}
Transmit first-order prediction residuals to exploit temporal redundancy. Define the prediction residual as
\begin{equation}
r_t^{(c)} = x_t^{(c)} - x_{t-T_S}^{(c)}.
\label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}
Since $|r_t^{(c)}| \ll |x_t^{(c)}|$ for slowly varying pollutants, residuals can be quantized with a narrower dynamic range $[a_r^{(c)}, b_r^{(c)}]$ and fewer bits $b_r < b_c$. The quantized residual is
\begin{equation}
\tilde{r}_t^{(c)} = Q_r(r_t^{(c)}; a_r^{(c)}, b_r^{(c)}, b_r),
\label{eq:residual_quantization}
\end{equation}
and the reconstructed absolute value at the receiver is
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_t^{(c)} = \hat{x}_{t-T_S}^{(c)} + \tilde{r}_t^{(c)}.
\label{eq:residual_reconstruction}
\end{equation}

Empirically, residual ranges $[a_r^{(c)}, b_r^{(c)}] = [-3\sigma_c, +3\sigma_c]$, where $\sigma_c$ is the standard deviation of $r_t^{(c)}$ over training data, capture 99.7\% of residuals and enable $b_r = 4$ bits compared to $b_c = 6-8$ bits for absolute values. To prevent drift accumulation from quantization errors, an absolute reference is transmitted every $N_{\text{ref}} = 15$ packets ($\approx 60$ seconds), resetting the prediction chain \cite{przystupa2025ensuring}.

\subsection{Temporal Convolutional Network Architecture}

\subsubsection{Design Motivation}

Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks have been widely applied to air quality forecasting but suffer from sequential computation bottlenecks that hinder parallel training and inference \cite{nguyen2025aiot}. Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs) address these limitations by employing dilated causal convolutions, which enable parallel processing, stable gradients, and flexible receptive fields \cite{bai2018tcn}. Recent work demonstrates that TCNs achieve competitive or superior accuracy compared to RNNs for time series prediction with significantly lower computational cost \cite{gunasekar2025power}. Furthermore, TCNs' convolutional structure is naturally compatible with hardware accelerators (e.g., ARM CMSIS-NN, TensorFlow Lite) and post-training quantization techniques \cite{mazinani2025air}.

\subsubsection{Dilated Causal Convolutions}

A causal convolution at layer $l$ ensures that the output at time $t$ depends only on inputs from time $t$ and earlier, preserving temporal ordering:
\begin{equation}
y_t^{(l)} = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} w_i^{(l)} \cdot x_{t-i}^{(l-1)},
\label{eq:causal_conv}
\end{equation}
where $k$ is the kernel size and $w_i^{(l)}$ are learnable weights. To expand the receptive field without increasing parameters, dilated convolutions introduce spacing $d$ (dilation factor) between kernel taps:
\begin{equation}
y_t^{(l)} = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} w_i^{(l)} \cdot x_{t - d \cdot i}^{(l-1)}.
\label{eq:dilated_conv}
\end{equation}

For a stack of $n$ layers with dilation factors $d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n$ and kernel size $k$, the receptive field (number of past timesteps influencing the output) is
\begin{equation}
R = 1 + n(k-1) \sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i.
\label{eq:receptive_field}
\end{equation}

Exponentially increasing dilations $d_i = 2^{i-1}$ enable receptive fields to grow exponentially with depth, capturing long-range dependencies efficiently \cite{bai2018tcn}.

\subsubsection{Residual Block Design}

Each TCN residual block consists of two dilated causal convolutional layers, each followed by weight normalization \cite{salimans2016weightnorm}, ReLU activation, and spatial dropout (rate 0.2) for regularization. The block structure is
\begin{align}
z_t^{(1)} &= \text{ReLU}\left( \text{Dropout}\left( \text{WeightNorm}\left( \text{Conv1D}_{d}(x_t) \right) \right) \right), \label{eq:tcn_block_1} \\
z_t^{(2)} &= \text{ReLU}\left( \text{Dropout}\left( \text{WeightNorm}\left( \text{Conv1D}_{d}(z_t^{(1)}) \right) \right) \right), \label{eq:tcn_block_2} \\
y_t &= z_t^{(2)} + \mathcal{F}(x_t), \label{eq:tcn_residual}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{F}(x_t)$ is a $1 \times 1$ convolution applied to the input $x_t$ if the number of channels changes between input and output, otherwise $\mathcal{F}(x_t) = x_t$ (identity mapping). Residual connections mitigate gradient vanishing and enable training of deeper networks \cite{he2016resnet}.

\subsubsection{Proposed TCN Configuration}

The proposed TCN architecture for DALTON air quality forecasting is specified in Table~\ref{tab:tcn_architecture}. The network comprises:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Input Layer}: Accepts quantized multi-pollutant sequences of shape $(L, C_{\text{in}})$, where $L = 256$ is the sequence length (256 seconds at 1~Hz, or 64 samples at 4~s subsampling) and $C_{\text{in}} = 6$ is the number of pollutant channels.

\item \textbf{Four Residual Blocks}: With exponentially increasing dilations $d \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$, kernel size $k = 3$, and $C_{\text{hidden}} = 32$ channels per block. This configuration yields a receptive field of
\begin{equation}
R = 1 + 4(3-1)(1 + 2 + 4 + 8) = 1 + 8 \cdot 15 = 121 \text{ timesteps},
\end{equation}
sufficient to capture dependencies over $\approx 2$ minutes of historical data.

\item \textbf{Global Average Pooling}: Aggregates temporal information into a fixed-length feature vector of dimension $C_{\text{hidden}} = 32$.

\item \textbf{Fully Connected Output Layer}: Maps the aggregated features to a scalar output $\hat{y}_t \in \mathbb{R}$ representing the predicted 10-minute ahead PM$_{2.5}$ concentration.
\end{itemize}

The total parameter count is approximately 38,000, enabling deployment on edge devices with $<$1~MB flash memory. For comparison, a comparable LSTM baseline with 2 layers and 64 hidden units requires $\approx$150,000 parameters and exhibits 2-3$\times$ higher inference latency on Raspberry Pi 4 \cite{mazinani2025air}.

\subsection{Hardware-Aware Training and Quantization}

\subsubsection{Quantization-Aware Training}

To ensure robustness to input quantization noise, we employ \textit{quantization-aware training} (QAT) \cite{jacob2018qat}. During training, quantization operations $Q_c(\cdot)$ defined in Equation~\eqref{eq:quantization} are inserted into the forward pass, simulating the effect of reduced precision. Specifically, raw training samples $x_t^{(c)}$ are quantized on-the-fly to $\tilde{x}_t^{(c)}$ before feeding into the TCN, while gradients flow through a straight-through estimator (STE) \cite{bengio2013ste} during backpropagation:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_t^{(c)}} \approx \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \tilde{x}_t^{(c)}}.
\label{eq:ste}
\end{equation}

This approach allows the network to learn representations that are resilient to quantization artifacts, such as staircase distortion and noise amplification at boundaries \cite{mazinani2025air}. We train separate models for each quantization configuration (e.g., 4-bit, 6-bit, mixed-precision) rather than using a single model with all configurations, as this specialization improves accuracy by 0.1-0.2 $\mu$g/m$^3$ RMSE.

\subsubsection{Post-Training Quantization of TCN Weights}

After training in full precision (FP32), the TCN model is quantized to INT8 using TensorFlow Lite's post-training quantization \cite{tensorflow2021ptq}. This reduces model size from $\approx$150~KB to $\approx$40~KB and accelerates inference on ARM-based edge devices through SIMD instructions. The quantization process maps FP32 weights $w$ to 8-bit integers:
\begin{equation}
w_{\text{int8}} = \text{round}\left( \frac{w - z_w}{s_w} \right), \quad w_{\text{int8}} \in [-128, 127],
\label{eq:weight_quantization}
\end{equation}
where $s_w$ is the scale factor and $z_w$ is the zero-point offset determined via calibration on a representative dataset. Activations are similarly quantized using per-layer scales and zero-points.

Empirical evaluation shows that INT8 quantization of TCN weights introduces $<$0.05 $\mu$g/m$^3$ RMSE degradation compared to FP32 inference, consistent with prior findings for convolutional models on air quality data \cite{mazinani2025air}.

\subsubsection{Hardware-Aware Neural Architecture Search}

While the TCN configuration in Table~\ref{tab:tcn_architecture} is manually designed based on domain knowledge and prior work, future iterations can leverage hardware-aware Neural Architecture Search (NAS) to co-optimize accuracy, latency, and energy consumption \cite{himeur2024edge}. A NAS framework for this application would define a search space over:

\begin{itemize}
\item Number of residual blocks $n_b \in \{2, 3, 4, 5\}$,
\item Dilation schedules (e.g., exponential, linear, custom),
\item Channel widths $C_{\text{hidden}} \in \{16, 32, 64\}$,
\item Kernel sizes $k \in \{3, 5, 7\}$,
\item Mixed-precision per-layer bit-widths.
\end{itemize}

The multi-objective reward function balances validation RMSE, estimated inference latency on target hardware (measured via a latency predictor or profiled lookup table), and memory footprint:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}(\theta) = w_1 \cdot \text{RMSE}(\theta) + w_2 \cdot \text{Latency}(\theta) + w_3 \cdot \text{Memory}(\theta),
\label{eq:nas_reward}
\end{equation}
where $\theta$ denotes architecture hyperparameters and $w_1, w_2, w_3$ are user-defined weights. Evolutionary algorithms or reinforcement learning agents (e.g., PPO, REINFORCE) can navigate this search space efficiently \cite{baker2016nas, zoph2017nas}.

For this work, we fix the TCN architecture to maintain experimental clarity and focus ablation studies on quantization strategies. NAS-based optimization is deferred to future work (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Energy Model for IoT Sensor Nodes}

\subsubsection{Model Components}

To quantify energy savings, we derive an explicit energy model partitioning node power consumption into sensing, computation, and communication components \cite{khan2024artificial}. Let:

\begin{itemize}
\item $E_{\text{sense}}^{(c)}$: Energy per sample for pollutant channel $c$ (dominated by gas sensor heating and ADC conversion), in Joules.
\item $E_{\text{MCU}}$: Energy per CPU cycle for the microcontroller (e.g., STM32, ESP32), in Joules/cycle.
\item $N_{\text{cycles}}^{\text{quant}}$: CPU cycles required to compute quantization (Equation~\eqref{eq:quantization}), range updates (Equation~\eqref{eq:adaptive_range}), and packet formatting.
\item $E_{\text{tx}}$: Energy per transmitted bit at the chosen radio power level and modulation scheme, in Joules/bit.
\item $b_{\text{payload}}$: Total payload bit-width per packet, equal to $\sum_{c=1}^{6} b_c$ for absolute quantization or $\sum_{c=1}^{6} b_r$ for residual quantization.
\item $b_{\text{overhead}}$: Protocol overhead (headers, checksums, addressing), typically 160-200 bits for LoRa or BLE packets \cite{przystupa2025ensuring}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Energy Per Packet and Average Power}

The energy consumed to transmit one quantized packet is
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{pkt}} = E_{\text{tx}} \cdot (b_{\text{payload}} + b_{\text{overhead}}).
\label{eq:packet_energy}
\end{equation}

Over a $T_S$-second interval, the sensor performs $T_S$ measurements (at 1~Hz) but transmits only once. Assuming constant sensing power and negligible sleep-mode energy, the average energy per second is
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{sec}} = \frac{1}{T_S} \left( T_S \sum_{c=1}^{6} E_{\text{sense}}^{(c)} + E_{\text{MCU}} \cdot N_{\text{cycles}}^{\text{quant}} + E_{\text{pkt}} \right).
\label{eq:energy_per_second}
\end{equation}

Simplifying and grouping constant terms:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{sec}} = \underbrace{\sum_{c=1}^{6} E_{\text{sense}}^{(c)}}_{\text{Sensing (constant)}} + \underbrace{\frac{E_{\text{MCU}} \cdot N_{\text{cycles}}^{\text{quant}}}{T_S}}_{\text{Computation}} + \underbrace{\frac{E_{\text{tx}} (b_{\text{payload}} + b_{\text{overhead}})}{T_S}}_{\text{Communication}}.
\label{eq:energy_decomposition}
\end{equation}

For low-power wireless transceivers (e.g., nRF52840 BLE, ESP32 Wi-Fi at low power), typical values are $E_{\text{tx}} = 30-50$ nJ/bit \cite{khan2024artificial}. Computation energy is often negligible compared to communication: $E_{\text{MCU}} \approx 0.1-0.5$ nJ/cycle and $N_{\text{cycles}}^{\text{quant}} \approx 10^3-10^4$ cycles/packet, yielding $E_{\text{MCU}} \cdot N_{\text{cycles}}^{\text{quant}} / T_S \approx 0.05-0.2$ $\mu$J/s, while $E_{\text{pkt}} / T_S \approx 1-10$ $\mu$J/s depending on $b_{\text{payload}}$ and $T_S$.

\subsection{Summary}

The proposed methodology integrates dynamic sensor-side quantization, temporal subsampling, and hardware-aware TCN design to address the energy-accuracy trade-off in IoT air quality monitoring. Key innovations include adaptive range scaling to minimize quantization noise (Equations~\eqref{eq:quantization}--\eqref{eq:adaptive_range}), residual encoding to exploit temporal correlation (Equations~\eqref{eq:residual}--\eqref{eq:residual_reconstruction}), a lightweight TCN architecture optimized for quantized inputs (Equations~\eqref{eq:dilated_conv}--\eqref{eq:tcn_residual}), and an explicit energy model enabling quantitative comparison of schemes (Equations~\eqref{eq:packet_energy}--\eqref{eq:energy_decomposition}). The next section describes the experimental design and validation on the DALTON dataset.
