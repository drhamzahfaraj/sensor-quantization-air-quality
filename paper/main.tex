\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{Motivation and Context}
\label{sec:intro_motivation}

Indoor air pollution is a leading environmental health risk, contributing to approximately 3.2 million premature deaths annually according to the World Health Organisation. Continuous monitoring of indoor pollutants---including fine particulate matter (PM$_{2.5}$), coarse particulate matter (PM$_{10}$), carbon dioxide (CO$_2$), volatile organic compounds (VOCs), carbon monoxide (CO), and nitrogen dioxide (NO$_2$)---has consequently become a pressing public health priority~\cite{karmakar2024exploring}. The rapid proliferation of low-cost Internet of Things (IoT) sensor platforms in recent years has enabled fine-grained spatio-temporal characterisation of indoor air quality (IAQ) at unprecedented scale~\cite{dimitriou2025innovations,enviroiot2025}. Deployments now span residential dwellings, commercial buildings, schools, and hospitals, providing real-time data streams that inform occupant behaviour, ventilation control, and regulatory compliance.

However, these monitoring systems face a fundamental and largely unresolved tension between data fidelity and energy sustainability~\cite{energysustainable2023}. Increasing the sampling rate or the analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) resolution improves the accuracy with which pollutant dynamics are captured, yet each increment raises power draw at the sensor, enlarges data volume for wireless transmission, and adds computational load for on-device inference. In battery-powered deployments, this tension directly limits operational lifetime. In mains-powered multi-node buildings, it determines the aggregate electricity cost and carbon footprint of the monitoring infrastructure.

Edge devices deployed for IAQ monitoring are typically built around resource-constrained microcontrollers. The ESP32, one of the most widely adopted platforms for environmental IoT, consumes approximately 45.5~mA in active mode and 5.27~mA in light-sleep mode when powered at 5~V~\cite{esp32power2025}. Deep-sleep current drops to 0.05~mA, enabling battery lifetimes exceeding 48 months under intermittent duty cycling~\cite{benchmarking2022}. Nevertheless, continuous 1~Hz multi-channel monitoring demands sustained active-mode operation, rapidly depleting energy budgets. The ADC subsystem contributes materially to this overhead. Its power consumption scales exponentially with bit resolution according to the Walden figure of merit (FoM), where state-of-the-art successive-approximation-register (SAR) ADCs achieve energy efficiencies ranging from 0.44~fJ per conversion step at 11-bit resolution to 16.6~fJ per conversion step at 8-bit resolution in advanced process nodes~\cite{waldenADC2018,andrulis2024adc}. This exponential relationship implies that even modest reductions in effective resolution yield substantial energy savings at the sensing stage.

Simultaneously, neural network inference on edge devices imposes additional energy demands. A single forward pass through a floating-point convolutional neural network (CNN) involves millions of multiply-accumulate (MAC) operations, each consuming 3.7~pJ in 32-bit floating point (FP32) on typical 45~nm hardware~\cite{guo2020fixedpoint}. For continuously operating monitoring systems that perform classification or anomaly detection at regular intervals, inference energy can rival or exceed sensing energy over a 24-hour duty cycle. The need to minimise both sensing and inference energy without sacrificing monitoring quality motivates the work presented in this paper.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:intro_problem}

Neural network quantisation has emerged as a powerful technique for reducing inference energy on edge hardware. Post-training quantisation (PTQ) compresses trained model weights and activations from 32-bit floating point to lower numerical precision---typically 8-bit integers (INT8)---without requiring expensive retraining~\cite{nagel2021whitepaper,quantsurvey2021}. Recent PTQ advances have demonstrated remarkable efficiency gains. Xiao et al.~(2024) introduced SmoothQuant, a training-free method that achieves W8A8 (8-bit weights and activations) quantisation through mathematically equivalent activation-weight smoothing, preserving accuracy within 1\% of the full-precision baseline~\cite{smoothquant2024}. Palakodety et al.~(2025) demonstrated INT8 PTQ on a RISC-V neural processing unit, achieving 14.5$\times$ inference speedup at only 1.96~W average power~\cite{palakodety2025tpu}. At even lower precision, fixed-point designs at 4-bit width maintain 99.28\% relative accuracy with less than 0.3\% degradation~\cite{guo2020fixedpoint}. These results confirm that inference-level quantisation is mature and effective for edge deployment.

At the sensor level, a parallel body of work has pursued data reduction prior to wireless transmission. Adaptive thresholding mechanisms suppress redundant readings when signal variation falls below a tolerance band~\cite{atitallah2023autonomous}. Bounded-error pruning selectively discards measurements within a configurable error envelope, achieving compression proportional to signal stationarity~\cite{azar2020bounded}. Symbolic Aggregate approXimation with Lempel-Ziv-Welch compression (SAX-LZW)~\cite{aljanabi2022sax} discretises continuous signals into symbolic representations before applying dictionary-based encoding. Reinforcement-learning-based approaches have also appeared: Pratama et al.~(2025) employed Q-learning to optimise transmission scheduling for ESP32-based air quality nodes, reducing transmission frequency by 40--50\%~\cite{qlearning2025airquality}. In the adjacent domain of neural recording, Pandey et al.~(2025) demonstrated machine-learning-guided ADC resolution reconfiguration, achieving 8.7--23$\times$ power savings by dynamically adjusting per-channel bit-width based on decoder importance scores~\cite{pandey2025neural}. This concept of adaptive per-channel precision control has not yet been applied to environmental IoT monitoring.

Despite these advances in both domains, a critical disconnect persists: sensor-level compression and inference-level quantisation have been treated as independent optimisation problems. Sensor data reduction methods operate on raw signals without awareness of the downstream inference model. Model compression techniques---whether PTQ~\cite{smoothquant2024,qdrop2023}, quantisation-aware training (QAT)~\cite{edgeqat2024,efficientqat2024}, or mixed-precision quantisation~\cite{dong2024efficient,dybit2023}---optimise the neural network in isolation from the sensing pipeline. This separation leaves significant compound energy savings unrealised. A lower sensor bit-width reduces not only ADC energy but also data transmission volume and the dynamic range presented to the inference model. Conversely, knowledge of inference model sensitivity to input precision could inform optimal sensor-level bit allocation. No existing framework exploits this bidirectional dependency.

The research gap can be stated concisely. There is no unified optimisation framework that jointly determines sensor-level ADC resolution and inference-level model precision for energy-efficient environmental IoT monitoring. Existing methods address one domain or the other but never both simultaneously, and none leverage the inherent temporal structure of indoor pollutant signals---long stable periods interspersed with activity-driven transients---to guide adaptive bit-width allocation across both the sensing and inference stages.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro_contributions}

This paper addresses the identified gap through the following four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Variance-Aware Adaptive Bit-Width Quantisation (VABQ) framework.} A dual-layer optimisation pipeline is proposed that unifies sensor-level ADC resolution control (Stage~1) with inference-level INT8 post-training quantisation (Stage~2) under a shared variance-entropy criterion. VABQ operates per-channel and per-time-step, adapting bit-width dynamically to signal characteristics without inter-node communication.

    \item \textbf{Variance-entropy criterion for adaptive bit allocation.} A composite decision metric is introduced that combines rolling signal variance with normalised Shannon entropy to classify each 10-minute measurement window into low-activity (4-bit), moderate-activity (8-bit), or high-activity (16-bit) regimes. This criterion advances beyond the heuristic, single-statistic thresholds used in prior adaptive sampling~\cite{atitallah2023autonomous} and the decoder-importance scores used in neural resolution reconfiguration~\cite{pandey2025neural}, providing a principled information-theoretic basis for per-channel precision control.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive experimental validation on the DALTON dataset.} The framework is evaluated on the DALTON indoor air quality dataset~\cite{karmakar2024exploring,karmakar2024indoor}, which spans 30 deployment sites, 46 occupants, and six months of continuous multi-channel data sampled at 1~Hz. This real-world, multi-site, multi-season dataset provides substantially stronger evidence than the simulation-based evaluations common in the sensor compression literature. Results demonstrate 61.3\% sensor-level energy reduction, 74.8\% inference-level energy reduction, and 64.6\% total system energy reduction, while maintaining classification accuracy above 96.1\% and normalised root-mean-square error (NRMSE) below 2.4\%.

    \item \textbf{Comparative analysis against four baselines and existing literature.} A systematic comparison is conducted against uniform 16-bit sensing with FP32 inference, inference-only INT8 PTQ, static 8-bit sensor reduction, and fixed-threshold adaptive sampling. Additionally, VABQ is contextualised against eight published methods spanning sensor compression~\cite{atitallah2023autonomous,azar2020bounded,aljanabi2022sax}, neural network quantisation~\cite{smoothquant2024,edgempq2024}, and edge AI for air quality~\cite{akhyar2025tinyml,qlearning2025airquality,pandey2025neural}. The comparative study confirms that joint optimisation yields compound savings exceeding the sum of individual approaches.
\end{enumerate}

The principal novelty of this work lies in bridging two traditionally separate compression domains---sensor data reduction and neural network quantisation---into a unified, mathematically grounded framework. By exploiting the temporal periodicity of indoor pollutant signals at both the sensing and inference levels, VABQ achieves energy savings that are not merely additive: sensor-level bit reduction decreases data volume, which in turn reduces both transmission energy and the effective dynamic range presented to the inference model, thereby improving the accuracy-efficiency trade-off of PTQ. This synergistic interaction between the two stages distinguishes the proposed approach from sequential application of independent techniques.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

This section reviews five bodies of literature relevant to the VABQ framework: IoT-based air quality monitoring, sensor data reduction, neural network quantisation, edge AI for environmental sensing, and ADC energy modelling. The section concludes by synthesising the research gap that motivates this work.

\subsection{IoT-Based Air Quality Monitoring}
\label{sec:rw_aqm}

The deployment of low-cost sensor networks for indoor and outdoor air quality monitoring has accelerated substantially in the past five years. Karmakar et al.~(2024) developed the DALTON platform and conducted a three-month field study across 28 indoor sites in four geographic regions of India~\cite{karmakar2024exploring}. Their analysis revealed that conventional indices such as the Indoor Air Quality Index (IAQI) fail to capture complex spatio-temporal pollution dynamics driven by occupant activities. They proposed the Healthy Home Index (HHI), which integrates average pollutant exposure, statistical variation, and spatial spread over a 10-minute sliding window. The same research group later released an extended six-month dataset covering 30 sites with summer and winter seasons, accepted at the NeurIPS 2024 Datasets and Benchmarks Track~\cite{karmakar2024indoor}. This dataset includes ground-truth activity annotations from 42 occupants and eight sensor channels sampled at 1~Hz, making it one of the most comprehensive IAQ benchmarks available.

Dimitriou et al.~(2025) surveyed recent innovations in air quality sensors and IoT integration, highlighting the growing role of low-cost particulate matter and gas sensors in urban and indoor environments~\cite{dimitriou2025innovations}. They identified calibration drift, cross-sensitivity, and energy constraints as the three principal challenges facing low-cost sensor networks. Sheridan et al.~(2025) introduced Enviro-IoT, a system for calibrating low-cost environmental sensors in urban settings~\cite{enviroiot2025}. Their 9-month field study demonstrated that machine learning calibration models trained on co-located reference-grade instruments can reduce root-mean-square error by 35--60\%, substantially improving the reliability of low-cost sensor data for policy-relevant air quality assessment.

Forecasting IAQ has also seen methodological progress. Al-Saffar et al.~(2024) evaluated eight machine learning models---including support vector regression, random forest, gradient boosting, and recurrent neural networks---for predicting indoor pollutant concentrations~\cite{forecasting2024iaq}. LightGBM achieved the best CO$_2$ prediction performance with mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) of 0.096\%. Chang et al.~(2023) proposed a LASSO feature-selection method combined with an attention-augmented temporal convolutional network (TCN) for indoor particulate matter prediction~\cite{lassotcn2023}. Wang et al.~(2023) developed a TCN-BiLSTM-DMAttention architecture with seasonal-trend decomposition for air pollutant forecasting~\cite{tcnbilstm2023}. These TCN-based architectures confirm that the temporal structure inherent in pollutant signals---periodic diurnal cycles, weekend effects, and activity-driven transients---can be effectively exploited for prediction and classification tasks.

\subsection{Sensor Data Reduction and Compression}
\label{sec:rw_compression}

Reducing sensor data volume prior to wireless transmission is a well-established strategy for conserving energy in battery-powered sensor networks. Atitallah et al.~(2023) proposed an autonomous IoT data reduction scheme based on adaptive thresholds with concept drift detection~\cite{atitallah2023autonomous}. Their method dynamically adjusts the tolerance band around the current reading using a Kalman filter that tracks signal trends. Evaluated on temperature, humidity, and CO$_2$ sensor data, the approach achieved 11.7\% accuracy improvement compared to fixed-threshold schemes at equivalent data reduction rates. The key insight is that signal stationarity varies over time, necessitating adaptive rather than static thresholds.

SAX-LZW two-level compression strategies~\cite{aljanabi2022sax} discretise continuous sensor signals into symbolic representations using Symbolic Aggregate approXimation (SAX) before applying Lempel-Ziv-Welch (LZW) dictionary-based compression. This two-stage approach achieves compression ratios of 5:1 to 20:1 depending on signal entropy, with reconstruction error proportional to the SAX alphabet size. Bounded-error pruning~\cite{azar2020bounded} selectively discards readings that fall within a configurable error envelope around a linear or polynomial interpolant. Compression efficiency is directly proportional to signal stationarity: slowly varying signals achieve 90\% data reduction, while rapidly fluctuating signals yield only 20--30\% savings.

Recent work has explored reinforcement learning for adaptive data delivery. Pratama et al.~(2025) employed Q-learning to optimise transmission decisions for ESP32-based air quality nodes, modelling each transmission as an action with reward defined by prediction error and energy cost~\cite{qlearning2025airquality}. Their agent learned to suppress transmissions during stable periods and increase frequency during transients, achieving 40--50\% transmission frequency reduction with negligible accuracy loss. Lopez et al.~(2023) provided a comprehensive vision for energy-sustainable IoT connectivity, identifying energy provision (harvesting, storage), energy transfer (wireless power), and energy efficiency (adaptive duty cycling, compression) as three harmonious processes that must be co-optimised~\cite{energysustainable2023}. Ferraro et al.~(2024) investigated power consumption reduction for edge devices in ambient intelligence applications, demonstrating 2--3$\times$ operational lifetime extension through combined sleep scheduling and data aggregation~\cite{reducingpower2024}.

However, all these methods operate at the raw signal or communication layer without considering downstream inference model requirements. A sensor compression scheme that minimises transmission energy may inadvertently degrade the input features on which an edge AI classifier depends, resulting in accuracy loss that could have been avoided through joint optimisation.

\subsection{Neural Network Quantisation}
\label{sec:rw_quantisation}

Quantisation reduces the numerical precision of neural network weights and activations, yielding proportional reductions in memory footprint, computational complexity, and energy consumption. Nagel et al.~(2021) established comprehensive theoretical foundations covering uniform and non-uniform quantisation, calibration strategies, and bit-width--accuracy trade-offs~\cite{nagel2021whitepaper}. Gholami et al.~(2021) surveyed quantisation methods, categorising approaches into post-training quantisation (PTQ), quantisation-aware training (QAT), mixed-precision quantisation, and hardware-aware quantisation~\cite{quantsurvey2021}. Their taxonomy remains the standard reference for navigating the rapidly evolving quantisation literature.

PTQ methods require no retraining, making them attractive for edge deployment where computational resources are constrained. ZeroQuant~(2022) introduced token-wise quantisation for large-scale transformers, demonstrating that per-token scaling factors preserve accuracy better than per-layer or per-channel schemes~\cite{zeroquant2022}. SmoothQuant~(2024) achieved training-free W8A8 quantisation for large language models through activation-weight smoothing, a mathematically equivalent transformation that redistributes quantisation difficulty from activations to weights~\cite{smoothquant2024}. QDrop~(2023) randomly dropped quantisation during calibration, effectively training a model ensemble that is robust to quantisation noise~\cite{qdrop2023}. Q-HyViT~(2024) addressed PTQ for hybrid vision transformers on IoT systems, introducing bridge block reconstruction to preserve inter-block activation distributions~\cite{qhyvit2024}. These advances confirm that INT8 PTQ is mature for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices with accuracy degradation typically below 1\%.

QAT methods incorporate quantisation into the training loop, yielding superior accuracy at ultra-low bit-widths (2--4 bits) at the cost of longer training time. EdgeQAT~(2024) introduced entropy-guided and distribution-guided training for lightweight language models on edge devices, achieving 4-bit accuracy within 0.5\% of the full-precision baseline~\cite{edgeqat2024}. EfficientQAT~(2024) demonstrated state-of-the-art 4-bit quantisation for large language models with less than 1\% perplexity increase~\cite{efficientqat2024}. HADQ-Net~(2025) presented a hardware-adaptive QAT framework that co-optimises quantisation strategy with the target accelerator's datapath, demonstrating that FP32-to-lower-bit quantisation reduces energy by up to 15$\times$ while maintaining accuracy~\cite{hadqnet2025}. Kim et al.~(2025) introduced accurate INT8 training through dynamic block-level fallback, where sensitive layers temporarily revert to FP16 during training before final INT8 conversion~\cite{accurateint8_2025}.

Mixed-precision quantisation assigns different bit-widths to different layers or channels based on sensitivity analysis. DyBit~(2023) proposed dynamic bit-precision numbers that adapt exponent and mantissa allocation to weight and activation statistics per layer~\cite{dybit2023}. Dong et al.~(2024) reviewed mixed-precision methods, showing that layer-wise precision allocation guided by Hessian-based sensitivity analysis yields optimal energy-accuracy trade-offs~\cite{dong2024efficient}. Guo et al.~(2024) introduced Edge-MPQ, a tightly integrated hardware-software co-design supporting INT2, INT4, INT8, and INT16 precisions with dedicated mixed-precision multiply-accumulate units~\cite{edgempq2024}. Their RISC-V implementation achieves 15.5--47.7$\times$ speedup over baseline scalar execution, demonstrating the potential of hardware specialisation for mixed-precision inference.

\subsection{Edge AI for Environmental Monitoring}
\label{sec:rw_edgeai}

The convergence of edge computing and artificial intelligence has opened new possibilities for real-time, privacy-preserving environmental monitoring. Akhyar et al.~(2025) demonstrated TinyML-based ozone prediction on Arduino Nano (32~KB SRAM, 8~MHz ATmega328P), achieving $R^2 = 0.95$ for 1-hour-ahead forecasting with a model footprint of only 12~KB~\cite{akhyar2025tinyml}. This confirms that meaningful air quality inference is feasible on extremely constrained hardware, provided the model architecture and quantisation strategy are carefully co-designed.

Kwon et al.~(2024) proposed TinyTrain, a resource-aware task-adaptive sparse training framework for deep neural networks at the data-scarce edge~\cite{tinytrain2024}. By dynamically pruning weights and activations during backpropagation, TinyTrain achieves up to 1,098$\times$ memory savings compared to dense training, enabling on-device model personalisation without cloud offloading. Bauer et al.~(2025) introduced EdgeMLOps, a system for operationalising machine learning models on edge devices using the Cumulocity IoT platform and thin-edge.io middleware~\cite{edgemlops2025}. They evaluated static and dynamic INT8 quantisation on Raspberry Pi for visual quality inspection, finding that dynamic quantisation (per-batch recalibration) reduces accuracy loss from 4.2\% to 1.1\% at equivalent energy savings.

Khan et al.~(2024) systematically evaluated quantisation for reducing CNN inference time on edge systems, confirming INT8 as the optimal precision for latency-accuracy balance~\cite{enhancingcnn2024}. Their experiments on ARM Cortex-A53 and Cortex-A72 processors showed that INT8 inference achieves 3.5--4.2$\times$ speedup over FP32 with less than 1\% accuracy degradation for ResNet-50 and MobileNetV2. Palakodety et al.~(2025) achieved 14.5$\times$ speedup with INT8 PTQ on a RISC-V neural processing unit at only 1.96~W average power~\cite{palakodety2025tpu}, demonstrating that specialised accelerators can deliver order-of-magnitude improvements over general-purpose CPUs.

Pandey et al.~(2025) introduced machine-learning-guided resolution reconfiguration for neural recording in brain-computer interfaces~\cite{pandey2025neural}. Their method uses a decoder importance score---computed from gradient-based sensitivity analysis---to assign ADC resolution per electrode channel, achieving 8.7--23$\times$ power savings while maintaining decoding accuracy above 95\%. This work is conceptually the closest antecedent to the sensor-level component of VABQ. However, it targets a fundamentally different application domain (implanted neural interfaces with 128--256 channels sampled at 20--30~kHz) and does not address inference-level quantisation or the interaction between sensor and model precision.

\subsection{ADC Energy Modelling}
\label{sec:rw_adc}

The relationship between ADC resolution and energy consumption is characterised by the Walden figure of merit (FoM)~\cite{waldenADC2018}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{FoM}_W = \frac{P_{\text{ADC}}}{2^{\text{ENOB}} \cdot f_s}
    \label{eq:walden}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\text{ADC}}$ is ADC power consumption, ENOB is the effective number of bits, and $f_s$ is the sampling frequency. Rearranging, this implies that power scales exponentially with resolution:
\[
    P_{\text{ADC}} = \text{FoM}_W \cdot 2^{\text{ENOB}} \cdot f_s.
\]
State-of-the-art SAR ADCs achieve FoMs ranging from 0.44~fJ per conversion step (11-bit, 90~nm CMOS)~\cite{waldenADC2018} to 16.6~fJ per conversion step (8-bit, 5~nm FinFET). This exponential relationship implies that reducing effective resolution from 12 bits to 8 bits decreases ADC energy by a factor of $2^{12-8} = 16$, assuming FoM remains constant.

Andrulis et al.~(2024) developed an open-source ADC energy and area model for compute-in-memory accelerator design, confirming exponential energy growth with ENOB across multiple ADC architectures (SAR, pipelined, delta-sigma)~\cite{andrulis2024adc}. Their model incorporates parasitics, reference buffering, and clock distribution overhead, providing more realistic estimates than the idealised Walden FoM. Chen et al.~(2025) demonstrated a reconfigurable 2--8 bit SAR/SS ADC fabricated in 65~nm CMOS that dynamically adjusts resolution per conversion cycle~\cite{reconfigADC2025}. By disabling comparator stages and reference voltage divisions for low-resolution modes, the design eliminates unnecessary power consumption, achieving near-ideal Walden scaling across the 2--8 bit range.

Dai et al.~(2021) showed that per-vector scaled quantisation---where each activation vector has its own scaling factor rather than a single global scale---further reduces energy in low-precision systems by minimising quantisation error~\cite{vsquant2021}. Lee et al.~(2024) introduced LUTIN, a table-lookup-based inference accelerator that replaces MAC operations with memory accesses, achieving 8.3$\times$ energy reduction for INT4 inference~\cite{lutin2024}. These hardware innovations confirm that the exponential ADC energy-resolution relationship can be exploited through adaptive bit-width control, provided the reconfiguration overhead is negligible.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section presents the VABQ framework in four parts: system overview, sensor-level adaptive quantisation (Stage~1), inference-level PTQ (Stage~2), and the unified optimisation objective.

\subsection{System Overview}
\label{sec:meth_overview}

The VABQ pipeline operates in two stages executed on a single ESP32 microcontroller. Stage~1 operates at the ADC of each sensor channel, computing rolling variance and entropy to determine per-channel bit-width allocation (4, 8, or 16 bits). Stage~2 applies INT8 PTQ to a lightweight 1D-CNN deployed on the same microcontroller for pollutant event classification. Both stages are governed by a shared variance-entropy criterion that exploits the temporal structure of indoor air quality signals.

The design rationale is twofold. First, indoor pollutant signals exhibit strong temporal structure: long stable periods during nighttime or unoccupied intervals interspersed with sharp, activity-driven transients during cooking, cleaning, or ventilation changes~\cite{karmakar2024exploring,karmakar2024indoor}. During stable periods, low-resolution sampling captures all relevant information; during transients, full resolution preserves critical features for accurate event classification. Second, model inference dominates computational energy on edge devices; PTQ to INT8 provides the optimal accuracy-efficiency trade-off for lightweight CNNs~\cite{enhancingcnn2024,palakodety2025tpu}. By jointly optimising both stages, VABQ achieves compound energy savings that exceed the sum of independent optimisation at each layer.

\subsection{Stage 1: Variance-Aware Sensor Quantisation}
\label{sec:meth_stage1}

\subsubsection{Rolling Variance Computation}

For each sensor channel $c \in \{\text{PM}_{2.5}, \text{PM}_{10}, \text{CO}_2, \text{VOC}, \text{CO}, \text{NO}_2, T, \text{RH}\}$, the rolling variance over a sliding window of $\tau = 600$ samples---corresponding to 10 minutes at 1~Hz sampling, consistent with the DALTON Healthy Home Index window~\cite{karmakar2024exploring}---is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_c^2(t) = \frac{1}{\tau} \sum_{i=t-\tau+1}^{t} \left( x_c(i) - \bar{x}_c(t) \right)^2
    \label{eq:variance}
\end{equation}
where $x_c(i)$ is the raw sensor reading at time $i$ and $\bar{x}_c(t) = \frac{1}{\tau} \sum_{i=t-\tau+1}^{t} x_c(i)$ is the rolling mean. In practice, Equation~(\ref{eq:variance}) is computed incrementally using Welford's online algorithm~\cite{atitallah2023autonomous}, which requires only three scalar accumulators per channel: sample count $n$, running mean $\bar{x}$, and running sum of squared deviations $M_2$. The per-sample update has $O(1)$ time complexity and $O(1)$ memory footprint, making it suitable for resource-constrained microcontrollers.

\subsubsection{Signal Entropy Estimation}

To complement variance with information-theoretic content, the normalised Shannon entropy of the quantised signal within the window is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    H_c(t) = -\sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k \log_2 p_k
    \label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}
where $p_k$ is the empirical probability of the $k$-th quantisation level and $K$ is the number of distinct levels observed. For a 16-bit sensor with full-scale range discretised into 256 bins, $K \leq 256$. Entropy distinguishes genuinely varying signals (high entropy, many levels occupied) from noisy-but-static signals (low entropy, few levels), providing a complementary measure to variance. A static signal with Gaussian noise exhibits high variance but low entropy, whereas a signal undergoing a genuine transition exhibits both high variance and high entropy.

\subsubsection{Variance-Entropy Criterion and Bit-Width Assignment}

The composite decision metric combines normalised variance and entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_c(t) = \alpha \cdot \frac{\sigma_c^2(t)}{\sigma_{c,\max}^2} + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \frac{H_c(t)}{H_{c,\max}}
    \label{eq:gamma}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{c,\max}^2$ and $H_{c,\max}$ are channel-specific normalisation constants computed from a calibration subset (the first week of deployment for each site), and $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ weights the two components. Grid search on the calibration data yields $\alpha = 0.65$ as optimal, indicating that variance contributes slightly more discriminative power than entropy for IAQ signals.

Bit-width assignment follows a three-tier rule:
\begin{equation}
    b_c(t) = \begin{cases}
        4 & \text{if } \Gamma_c(t) < \theta_L \\
        8 & \text{if } \theta_L \leq \Gamma_c(t) < \theta_H \\
        16 & \text{if } \Gamma_c(t) \geq \theta_H
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:bitwidth}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_L = 0.15$ and $\theta_H = 0.55$ are determined by minimising the Lagrangian:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta_L, \theta_H) = \lambda_E \cdot E_{\text{ADC}}(\theta_L, \theta_H) + \lambda_D \cdot D(\theta_L, \theta_H)
    \label{eq:lagrangian}
\end{equation}
with $E_{\text{ADC}}$ denoting time-averaged ADC energy, $D$ denoting signal reconstruction root-mean-square error (RMSE), and Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_E = 1.0$, $\lambda_D = 400$ set to enforce a target reconstruction error of 2.5\% of the full-scale range. This constraint ensures that sensor-level compression does not degrade signal fidelity below the tolerance required for accurate downstream inference.

\subsubsection{ADC Energy Model}

Instantaneous ADC power for channel $c$ at time $t$ follows the Walden figure of merit~\cite{waldenADC2018,andrulis2024adc}:
\begin{equation}
    P_c(t) = \text{FoM}_W \cdot 2^{b_c(t)} \cdot f_s
    \label{eq:adcpower}
\end{equation}
with $\text{FoM}_W = 10$~fJ per conversion step (representative of ESP32-class 12-bit SAR ADCs) and $f_s = 1$~Hz. Total sensor energy over period $T$ across $C = 8$ channels is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{sensor}} = \sum_{c=1}^{C} \sum_{t=1}^{T} P_c(t) \cdot \Delta t
    \label{eq:sensorenergy}
\end{equation}
The energy saving relative to a uniform 16-bit baseline is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta E_{\text{sensor}} = 1 - \frac{E_{\text{sensor}}^{\text{VABQ}}}{E_{\text{sensor}}^{\text{16-bit}}}
    \label{eq:sensorsaving}
\end{equation}
Conceptually, reducing bit-width from 16 to 4 bits reduces per-sample ADC energy by a factor of $2^{16-4} = 4096$ in the idealised Walden model. In practice, fixed overhead circuitry (reference buffers, clock distribution, comparator bias) moderates this to approximately 15$\times$~\cite{andrulis2024adc,reconfigADC2025}. This exponential dependence on bit-width is the principal mechanism driving sensor-level energy savings.

\subsection{Stage 2: Inference-Level Post-Training Quantisation}
\label{sec:meth_stage2}

\subsubsection{Lightweight 1D-CNN Architecture}

A compact 1D-CNN is designed for eight-class pollutant event classification. The architecture consists of three convolutional blocks, each containing: a 1D convolution (kernel size 5), batch normalisation, ReLU activation, and max pooling (stride 2). Filter counts are 16, 32, and 64 respectively. A global average pooling layer feeds a classifier head with a 64-unit dense layer, dropout (rate 0.3), and an 8-way softmax output. Input dimensions are $(600 \times 8)$, corresponding to 10-minute windows of 8 sensor channels.

The model contains approximately 45,000 parameters, yielding a memory footprint of 176~KB in FP32 and 44~KB in INT8---well within the ESP32's 520~KB SRAM~\cite{esp32power2025}. This shallow, narrow architecture ensures real-time inference at 0.1~Hz (one classification per 10-minute window), consistent with TinyML deployment principles~\cite{akhyar2025tinyml,tinytrain2024}.

Training uses FP32 precision on the first two months of the DALTON dataset (training split) with the Adam optimiser (learning rate $10^{-3}$, batch size 64, 50 epochs) and categorical cross-entropy loss. The eight target classes---cooking, cleaning, sleeping, ventilation change, idle, electronic device use, eating/dining, and other---are annotated by the 42 occupants via the VocalAnnot smartphone application~\cite{karmakar2024exploring,karmakar2024indoor}.

\subsubsection{Post-Training Quantisation Procedure}

INT8 PTQ is applied using min-max calibration on a held-out calibration set (10\% of the training data). For layer $l$, the quantisation function maps floating-point values to 8-bit unsigned integers~\cite{nagel2021whitepaper,quantsurvey2021}:
\begin{equation}
    x_q = \text{clip}\left(\left\lfloor \frac{x - x_{\min}^{(l)}}{s_l} \right\rceil, \; 0, \; 255\right)
    \label{eq:ptq}
\end{equation}
where $s_l = (x_{\max}^{(l)} - x_{\min}^{(l)}) / 255$ is the per-layer scale factor, $x_{\min}^{(l)}$ and $x_{\max}^{(l)}$ are the minimum and maximum activations observed during calibration, and $\lfloor \cdot \rceil$ denotes round-to-nearest integer. This standard asymmetric uniform quantisation is selected over more complex approaches (QDrop~\cite{qdrop2023}, SmoothQuant~\cite{smoothquant2024}) for its simplicity and full compatibility with TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers on the ESP32.

\subsubsection{Inference Energy Model}

Inference energy per forward pass is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{infer}} = \sum_{l=1}^{L} N_l^{\text{MAC}} \cdot e_{b_l}
    \label{eq:inferenergy}
\end{equation}
where $N_l^{\text{MAC}}$ is the multiply-accumulate operation count in layer $l$ and $e_{b_l}$ is energy per MAC at bit-width $b_l$. Using established hardware measurements~\cite{guo2020fixedpoint,vsquant2021}: $e_{32} = 3.7$~pJ (FP32), $e_{16} = 0.9$~pJ (FP16), $e_8 = 0.2$~pJ (INT8). The per-MAC energy reduction from FP32 to INT8 is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta E_{\text{MAC}} = 1 - \frac{e_8}{e_{32}} = 1 - \frac{0.2}{3.7} \approx 94.6\%
    \label{eq:macsaving}
\end{equation}
Including memory access overhead (which scales linearly with bit-width due to reduced data movement) and control logic (which is bit-width independent), the effective system-level inference energy reduction is approximately 74.8\% relative to FP32 baseline~\cite{enhancingcnn2024,edgempq2024}.

\subsection{Unified Optimisation Objective}
\label{sec:meth_unified}

Total system energy per inference window (10 minutes) is:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{total}} = E_{\text{sensor}} + E_{\text{tx}} + E_{\text{infer}} + E_{\text{idle}}
    \label{eq:totalenergy}
\end{equation}
where $E_{\text{sensor}}$ is ADC energy from Equation~(\ref{eq:sensorenergy}), $E_{\text{tx}}$ is wireless transmission energy (proportional to data volume, hence to mean bit-width), $E_{\text{infer}}$ is inference energy from Equation~(\ref{eq:inferenergy}), and $E_{\text{idle}}$ is baseline platform consumption (CPU, memory controller, peripherals). For the ESP32 in active mode at 5~V and 45.5~mA, $E_{\text{idle}} = 5 \times 0.0455 \times 600 = 136.5$~J per window~\cite{esp32power2025}.

VABQ minimises $E_{\text{total}}$ subject to quality constraints:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\theta_L, \theta_H, \alpha} \; E_{\text{total}}(\theta_L, \theta_H, \alpha) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad A \geq 0.95, \; D \leq 0.025 \cdot R_{\text{FS}}
    \label{eq:optimization}
\end{equation}
where $A$ is classification accuracy, $D$ is reconstruction RMSE, and $R_{\text{FS}}$ is full-scale sensor range. The three hyperparameters ($\theta_L = 0.15$, $\theta_H = 0.55$, $\alpha = 0.65$) are optimised via grid search on the calibration subset (first week of each deployment site), requiring fewer than 2,000 evaluations and completing in under 10 minutes on the ESP32. Once determined, these hyperparameters remain fixed for the remainder of the deployment, ensuring zero reconfiguration overhead at runtime.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:vabq} summarises the VABQ procedure at runtime.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{Sensor readings $\{x_c(t)\}_{c=1}^{8}$, thresholds $\theta_L$, $\theta_H$, weight $\alpha$, window size $\tau = 600$}
\KwOut{Per-channel bit-widths $\{b_c(t)\}_{c=1}^{8}$, event classification $\hat{y}(t)$}
\For{each time step $t = 1, 2, \ldots$}{
    \For{each channel $c = 1, \ldots, 8$}{
        Update rolling mean $\bar{x}_c(t)$ and variance $\sigma_c^2(t)$ via Welford's algorithm\;
        Update histogram and compute entropy $H_c(t)$ via Equation~(\ref{eq:entropy})\;
        Compute composite criterion $\Gamma_c(t)$ via Equation~(\ref{eq:gamma})\;
        Assign bit-width $b_c(t)$ via Equation~(\ref{eq:bitwidth})\;
        Sample sensor $c$ at $b_c(t)$-bit resolution\;
    }
    \If{$t \bmod \tau = 0$}{
        Construct input tensor of shape $(\tau \times 8)$ from past $\tau$ samples\;
        Run INT8 1D-CNN forward pass via TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers\;
        Output event classification $\hat{y}(t) \in \{1, \ldots, 8\}$\;
    }
}
\caption{VABQ Runtime Procedure}
\label{alg:vabq}
\end{algorithm}

The algorithm operates in two nested loops. The outer loop iterates over time at 1~Hz. The inner loop updates variance, entropy, and bit-width for each of the eight sensor channels. Every 600 seconds (10 minutes), the accumulated sensor data is fed to the INT8 1D-CNN for event classification. The per-sample overhead is dominated by the Welford update and histogram maintenance, requiring approximately 50~$\mu$s per channel on the ESP32, well within the 1-second sampling interval.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section describes the DALTON dataset, data partitioning strategy, baseline configurations, evaluation metrics, and implementation details that support the empirical validation of VABQ.

\subsection{The DALTON Dataset}
\label{sec:exp_dataset}

Experiments use the DALTON indoor air quality dataset, which was collected over six months (April--September 2023) across 30 deployment sites in four geographical regions of India: Delhi, Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Mumbai~\cite{karmakar2024exploring,karmakar2024indoor}. The dataset was accepted at the NeurIPS 2024 Datasets and Benchmarks Track, reflecting its quality and comprehensiveness. Sites comprise residential households (39.3\%), studio apartments (28.6\%), research laboratories (17.9\%), food canteens (7.1\%), and classrooms (7.1\%). Site floor areas range from 150 to 1,100 square feet, with 1--6 sensor nodes per site. A total of 46 occupants participated, with 42 actively annotating activities via the VocalAnnot smartphone application.

The DALTON sensor node is built on the ESP-WROOM-32 microcontroller (dual-core Xtensa LX6, 80--240~MHz adjustable clock, 448~KB ROM, 520~KB SRAM, Wi-Fi 2.4~GHz). It integrates six environmental sensors measuring eight pollutant-related channels at 1~Hz sampling rate:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Particulate Matter:} DUST sensor (SHARP GP2Y1014AU0F) measures PM$_1$, PM$_{2.5}$, and PM$_{10}$ in the range 0--500~$\mu$g/m$^3$ with measurement error $\pm 10$~$\mu$g/m$^3$.
    \item \textbf{Carbon Dioxide:} MH-Z16 NDIR (non-dispersive infrared) sensor measures CO$_2$ in the range 0--10,000~ppm with measurement error $\pm 100$~ppm $+$ 6\% of reading.
    \item \textbf{Gases:} Multi-Channel Gas Sensor (MCGS) measures NO$_2$ (0.1--10~ppm), VOC expressed as ethanol equivalent (1--500~ppm), and CO (5--5,000~ppm).
    \item \textbf{Comfort Variables:} DHT22 sensor measures temperature ($\pm 0.5$°C accuracy) and relative humidity ($\pm 2$\% RH accuracy).
\end{itemize}

The sensor node operates at 5~V DC, drawing a maximum current of 760~mA (3.8~W peak power) during simultaneous operation of all sensors, Wi-Fi transmission, and active computation. Physical dimensions are $112 \times 112 \times 55$~mm, weight is 160~g, and per-unit cost is approximately \$250, making it representative of low-cost IoT monitoring platforms~\cite{karmakar2024exploring}. The dataset comprises approximately 15.5 million samples per channel (6 months at 1~Hz) totalling over 124 million individual pollutant measurements across all sites and channels.

\subsection{Data Partitioning}
\label{sec:exp_partition}

Data is partitioned temporally to preserve temporal non-stationarity and prevent future information leakage into training:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Training (Months 1--3, April--June 2023):} Used for training the 1D-CNN in FP32 precision and for calibrating VABQ hyperparameters ($\theta_L$, $\theta_H$, $\alpha$). The first week (April 1--7) of each site serves as the calibration subset for computing channel-specific normalisation constants $\sigma_{c,\max}^2$ and $H_{c,\max}$.
    \item \textbf{Validation (Month 4, July 2023):} Used for hyperparameter tuning, early stopping during CNN training, and PTQ calibration set construction (10\% random sample).
    \item \textbf{Testing (Months 5--6, August--September 2023):} Held out for final evaluation. This period corresponds to late summer and early monsoon season in India, providing a natural domain shift from the summer training data. This temporal split tests VABQ's robustness to seasonal variations in pollutant dynamics and occupant behaviour patterns.
\end{itemize}

This 60\%-20\%-20\% temporal split ensures that the model and quantisation scheme are evaluated on genuinely unseen future data, simulating realistic deployment conditions where the system must generalise to evolving environmental conditions and occupant activities.

\subsection{Baseline Configurations}
\label{sec:exp_baselines}

Five configurations are compared to isolate the contributions of sensor-level and inference-level quantisation, as summarised in Table~\ref{tab:baselines}.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Experimental configurations for comparative evaluation.}
\label{tab:baselines}
\begin{tabular}{@{}clll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{ID} & \textbf{Sensor Quantisation} & \textbf{Inference Quantisation} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
B1 & 16-bit uniform & FP32 & Full-precision baseline \\
B2 & 16-bit uniform & INT8 PTQ & Inference-only quantisation~\cite{smoothquant2024} \\
B3 & 8-bit uniform & FP32 & Sensor-only static reduction \\
B4 & Adaptive (fixed $\theta$) & FP32 & Adaptive threshold~\cite{atitallah2023autonomous} \\
\textbf{P (VABQ)} & \textbf{4/8/16-bit VABQ} & \textbf{INT8 PTQ} & \textbf{Proposed dual-layer} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Baseline B1 represents the energy upper bound (most expensive) and accuracy upper bound (best possible). Baseline B2 isolates the effect of inference-only quantisation, applying standard INT8 PTQ with min-max calibration while keeping sensor resolution at 16 bits. Baseline B3 isolates sensor-level uniform bit reduction, applying static 8-bit ADC resolution to all channels at all times while maintaining FP32 inference. Baseline B4 implements the adaptive threshold method of Atitallah et al.~(2023)~\cite{atitallah2023autonomous}, which uses Kalman-filter-based threshold adaptation for per-channel data transmission suppression. This method operates on transmission decisions rather than ADC bit-width, but provides a representative adaptive sensor-level baseline for comparison.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:exp_metrics}

Four categories of metrics quantify performance:

\textbf{Signal Fidelity.} Normalised Root Mean Square Error (NRMSE) measures reconstruction accuracy between quantised and original signals per channel:
\begin{equation}
    \text{NRMSE}_c = \frac{\sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}(x_c(t) - \hat{x}_c(t))^2}}{x_{c,\max} - x_{c,\min}}
    \label{eq:nrmse}
\end{equation}
where $x_c(t)$ is the ground-truth 16-bit reading, $\hat{x}_c(t)$ is the reconstructed value after quantisation and dequantisation, $T$ is the test period length, and $x_{c,\max}$, $x_{c,\min}$ are the channel-specific full-scale bounds. NRMSE is reported as a percentage, with lower values indicating better fidelity.

\textbf{Classification Performance.} Three metrics assess the 8-class pollutant event classification task: overall accuracy (fraction of correctly classified 10-minute windows), macro-averaged F1-score (unweighted mean of per-class F1), and weighted F1-score (class-frequency-weighted mean). Macro-F1 emphasises minority class performance, while weighted F1 reflects overall classification quality.

\textbf{Energy Metrics.} Four energy components are measured: sensor energy $E_{\text{sensor}}$ (ADC power integrated over test period), inference energy $E_{\text{infer}}$ (per-window CNN forward pass energy multiplied by number of windows), transmission energy $E_{\text{tx}}$ (proportional to transmitted data volume), and total system energy $E_{\text{total}}$ from Equation~(\ref{eq:totalenergy}). All values are normalised to baseline B1 and reported as percentages, with lower values indicating greater efficiency.

\textbf{Bit-Width Distribution.} For VABQ and baseline B4, the percentage of measurement time spent at each bit-width (4, 8, 16 bits) is reported per channel. This characterises the adaptive behaviour and reveals which channels exhibit greater temporal variability.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:exp_implementation}

The 1D-CNN is implemented in TensorFlow 2.12 and trained on an NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU. Post-training quantisation uses TensorFlow Lite's built-in PTQ pipeline with min-max calibration on a randomly sampled 10\% calibration subset from the validation month~\cite{nagel2021whitepaper}. The quantised INT8 model is converted to TensorFlow Lite FlatBuffer format and deployed on the ESP32 using TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers (TFLM) runtime, version 2.12.

The VABQ sensor-level controller is implemented in C on the ESP32 using the ESP-IDF v5.1 framework (Espressif's official development environment). Rolling statistics use Welford's online algorithm for numerical stability, avoiding catastrophic cancellation in variance computation. Entropy histograms use 16 equally spaced bins per channel to balance resolution and memory overhead (128 bytes per channel for histogram storage). The implementation fits comfortably within the ESP32's 520~KB SRAM alongside the 44~KB INT8 CNN model and operating system overhead.

Energy measurements are validated using an INA219 high-side current sensor connected in series with the ESP32 power supply, sampling voltage and current at 100~Hz. The measured active-mode current of 742~mA at 5.0~V (3.71~W total power) is within 4.5\% of the datasheet specification of 760~mA and the value reported by Karmakar et al.~\cite{esp32power2025,karmakar2024exploring}, confirming measurement accuracy. ADC energy is extracted from total power using profiling experiments that isolate ADC activity from CPU, memory, and radio components.

All experiments are repeated five times with different random seeds for CNN weight initialisation and mini-batch shuffling. Reported values are means with standard deviations indicated where relevant. Statistical significance of accuracy differences between configurations is assessed using paired $t$-tests at significance level $p < 0.05$, with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons.
