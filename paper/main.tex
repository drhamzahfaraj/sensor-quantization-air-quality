\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Indoor air pollution remains a leading environmental health risk, causing an estimated 3.2 million premature deaths annually, with disproportionate impacts on low- and middle-income communities where continuous monitoring is most needed yet least affordable. Battery-powered Internet of Things (IoT) sensor networks offer a scalable solution for real-time indoor air quality (IAQ) monitoring, but face a fundamental engineering trade-off: radio transmission of high-precision sensor readings dominates system power budgets, limiting deployment lifetimes to days rather than the months required for sustained public health surveillance. Existing approaches address either model-side quantization for edge inference acceleration or heuristic duty-cycling for power reduction, but no prior work jointly optimizes sensor-level data quantization, dynamic channel selection, and deep learning model compression within a unified energy--accuracy framework.

This paper presents a novel hybrid adaptive framework that integrates five synergistic components to address this gap. First, we introduce \textit{context-aware mixed-precision sensor quantization} with entropy-driven dynamic bit allocation, where quantization precision (2--8 bits) adapts per pollutant channel and time window based on signal variability computed over 64-second sliding windows. Second, we develop \textit{event-driven sampling triggers} that suppress redundant transmissions during quasi-static periods, reducing effective transmission rates by up to 40\%. Third, we propose \textit{SHAP-based dynamic sensor gating} that selectively disables low-importance channels at runtime based on battery state and environmental conditions, cutting sensing energy by 20--35\%. Fourth, we design a \textit{lightweight temporal convolutional network (TCN)} with four dilated causal convolutional blocks and mixed-precision INT8/INT4 layers, trained via joint quantization-aware training (QAT) with knowledge distillation from a full-precision teacher model. Fifth, we employ \textit{multi-objective Bayesian optimization} for offline policy search combined with \textit{lightweight Q-learning} for runtime adaptation to deployment-time variations including battery degradation and seasonal drift.

We validate the proposed framework using the DALTON indoor air quality dataset, comprising 89.1 million timestamped samples from 30 geographically and socioeconomically diverse residential sites across India, spanning 13,646 hours of continuous 1~Hz monitoring of PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$, CO$_2$, NO$_2$, VOC, and CO concentrations. Experiments on 10 representative sites with chronological train--test splits demonstrate that the proposed aggressive configuration achieves 58\% total system energy reduction (sensing + transmission + inference) while limiting PM$_{2.5}$ forecasting degradation to 9.7\% mean absolute error (MAE) increase relative to the full-precision baseline (2.94 vs.\ 2.68~$\mu$g/m$^3$). The conservative configuration achieves 44\% energy savings with only 4.9\% MAE degradation. Signal-to-quantization-noise ratio (SQNR) analysis confirms that the adaptive scheme maintains 43.8~dB reconstruction fidelity for the target PM$_{2.5}$ channel, with an average effective bit-width of 3.7 bits. Ablation studies across six experimental dimensions---entropy-driven allocation, adaptive range scaling, sensor gating, event triggers, QAT with distillation, and mixed-precision model layers---quantify individual contributions and validate statistical significance via bootstrap confidence intervals and paired $t$-tests across sites. These results establish a principled 2026 state-of-the-art methodology for deploying energy-efficient, privacy-preserving IoT sensor networks that extend battery lifetime from 12.5 to 29.9 days (a 2.4$\times$ improvement) without compromising public health monitoring utility.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Edge AI, dynamic quantization, sensor quantisation, temporal convolutional networks (TCN), energy-efficient IoT, wireless sensor networks (WSN), indoor air quality (IAQ), mixed-precision inference, Bayesian optimization, reinforcement learning

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Indoor air pollution is responsible for approximately 3.2 million premature deaths annually worldwide, with disproportionate impacts on low- and middle-income communities where solid fuel combustion, inadequate ventilation, and limited awareness exacerbate exposure risks~\cite{karmakar2024exploring}. Real-time monitoring of indoor air quality (IAQ) has emerged as a critical intervention for public health, enabling occupants to identify hazardous pollutant levels and modify behaviors or environmental controls accordingly~\cite{quang2025ai, wang2025indoor}. The proliferation of low-cost Internet of Things (IoT) sensor platforms has democratized access to continuous multi-pollutant measurements, yet these systems face fundamental constraints: battery-powered nodes must balance temporal resolution, measurement precision, and energy consumption to achieve practical deployment lifetimes~\cite{przystupa2025ensuring, petrica2026realtime}.

Conventional IoT air quality monitoring architectures transmit high-precision sensor readings (typically 10--16 bits per channel) at frequencies of 0.1--1 Hz, generating substantial communication overhead~\cite{lopes2025lowcost, bakare2026iot}. Radio transmission dominates the energy budget of wireless sensor nodes, often consuming 50--100 times more power than sensing or local computation~\cite{khan2024artificial}. This energy bottleneck becomes acute when dense sensor networks are deployed to capture spatial heterogeneity in large buildings or communities, where replacing batteries or providing wired power to hundreds of nodes is logistically prohibitive~\cite{zaid2025investigating, bagkis2025evolving}. Na\"ive solutions such as reducing sampling rates or coarse averaging sacrifice the temporal resolution necessary to detect transient pollution events, such as cooking emissions or infiltration from outdoor sources~\cite{karmakar2024indoor}.

Recent advances in edge artificial intelligence (AI) have demonstrated that deep learning models can be quantized and deployed on resource-constrained hardware with minimal accuracy degradation~\cite{mazinani2025air, fici2025sensors}. Post-training quantization of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and recurrent models has enabled on-device inference for air quality prediction with INT8 or even lower-bit representations~\cite{davoli2024edge}. However, existing work predominantly focuses on model-side quantization to accelerate inference, neglecting the equally critical problem of sensor-level data quantization and its impact on end-to-end system performance~\cite{himeur2024edge}. A few studies have explored transmission compression or adaptive sampling in generic IoT contexts, but they lack task-aware optimization tailored to the unique spatiotemporal dynamics of environmental pollutants~\cite{rojek2026impact, gunasekar2025power}.

\subsection{Research Gap and Motivation}

The central research gap addressed in this work is the absence of an integrated framework that jointly optimizes (i) context-aware mixed-precision sensor quantization, (ii) dynamic sensor gating and selective activation, (iii) multi-objective energy-accuracy optimization, and (iv) hardware-aware deep learning architectures with runtime adaptation for air quality forecasting. While DALTON and other recent datasets have provided unprecedented scale and diversity for IAQ modeling~\cite{karmakar2024indoor}, no prior study has systematically investigated entropy-driven dynamic bit allocation, event-triggered sampling, and reinforcement learning-based runtime policies for multi-pollutant sensor streams.

This paper proposes a novel hybrid adaptive framework combining context-aware mixed-precision quantization with temporal convolutional networks (TCNs) optimized for edge deployment. Our approach exploits signal entropy, variance, and temporal dynamics to adaptively allocate 2--8 bits per channel, coupled with dynamic sensor gating and event-driven triggers. The quantized streams are processed by a lightweight TCN deployed at an edge gateway with INT8/INT4 mixed-precision layers. We validate this scheme using the DALTON dataset comprising 89.1 million samples from 30 geographically diverse sites~\cite{karmakar2024indoor}.

\subsection{Contributions}

The primary contributions of this work are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Context-aware mixed-precision quantization}: We introduce entropy and variance-driven dynamic bit allocation (2--8 bits per channel) with event-triggered sampling, achieving 40--50\% payload reduction compared to fixed uniform quantization.

\item \textbf{Dynamic sensor gating framework}: We develop SHAP-based feature importance ranking and runtime sensor selection policies that disable low-importance channels during stable periods, reducing sensing energy by 20--35\%.

\item \textbf{Joint quantization-aware training}: We design quantization-aware TCN training with simulated sensor noise and mixed-precision INT8/INT4 layers, achieving $<$10\% MAE degradation compared to full-precision baselines.

\item \textbf{Multi-objective optimization}: We employ Bayesian optimization (offline) and lightweight Q-learning (runtime) for joint energy-accuracy optimization, establishing Pareto-optimal quantization policies.

\item \textbf{Comprehensive DALTON evaluation}: Using chronological train--test splits across 10 sites, we demonstrate 45--65\% total system energy reduction with explicit modeling of sensing, transmission, and inference costs.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{IoT-Based Air Quality Monitoring Systems}

The past decade has witnessed explosive growth in low-cost IoT platforms for air quality monitoring, driven by advances in miniaturized sensors, wireless communication modules, and cloud computing infrastructure~\cite{garcia2025advancements}. Peixe and Marques~\cite{peixe2024lowcost} conducted a systematic review of IoT-enabled IAQ systems, identifying key trends including the shift from standalone devices to networked multi-node deployments, integration of machine learning for predictive analytics, and challenges related to sensor calibration and data quality~\cite{kim2025machine}. Recent implementations leverage ESP32, Raspberry Pi, and Arduino platforms coupled with electrochemical, metal-oxide semiconductor (MOS), and optical particulate matter sensors to measure pollutants such as PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$, CO$_2$, NO$_2$, and volatile organic compounds (VOCs)~\cite{bakare2026iot, petrica2026realtime, mahmood2025monitoring}.

Several studies have emphasized energy efficiency as a critical design constraint. Przystupa et al.~\cite{przystupa2025ensuring} analyzed power consumption profiles of IoT-based IAQ monitors and proposed duty-cycling strategies to extend battery life, achieving up to 40\% energy savings by reducing active sensing and transmission periods. However, their approach does not address data quantization or compression, leaving substantial energy reduction potential untapped. Zaid et al.~\cite{zaid2025investigating} deployed a hyper-local IoT sensor network and demonstrated the feasibility of real-time exposure assessment, but transmission of full-resolution data limited scalability to dense deployments. Bagkis et al.~\cite{bagkis2025evolving} reviewed evolving trends in low-cost sensor networks and highlighted the need for intelligent data management strategies to handle the volume and velocity of streaming pollutant measurements, a challenge directly addressed by our quantization framework.

\subsection{Edge AI and Deep Learning for Air Quality Prediction}

The application of deep learning to air quality forecasting has progressed from cloud-centric models to edge-deployable architectures optimized for latency, privacy, and energy efficiency~\cite{fici2025sensors, himeur2024edge}. Mazinani et al.~\cite{mazinani2025air} recently demonstrated that quantized convolutional and recurrent neural networks deployed on embedded platforms (e.g., STM32, ESP32) can achieve prediction accuracy comparable to cloud-based counterparts while reducing inference latency and communication overhead. Their work focused on post-training quantization of trained models to INT8 and INT16 formats, validating deployment on microcontroller units with limited RAM and flash memory. However, they assumed availability of high-precision sensor inputs and did not investigate the impact of quantizing the input data stream itself.

Davoli et al.~\cite{davoli2024edge} proposed an edge computing-oriented Web of Things (WoT) architecture for mobile vehicular air quality monitoring, employing lightweight CNN models for real-time PM and ozone prediction. Their system achieved sub-second inference times on Raspberry Pi 4 devices using TensorFlow Lite, but energy analysis was limited to computational overhead and did not account for upstream transmission costs from sensors to edge gateways. Nguyen et al.~\cite{nguyen2025aiot} developed an AIoT-based IAQ prediction system integrating metaheuristic feature selection and hybrid deep learning (CNN--LSTM), reporting improved accuracy for 1-hour ahead forecasts. Their approach required extensive computational resources for hyperparameter tuning, which may not generalize to ultra-low-power edge scenarios.

Gunasekar et al.~\cite{gunasekar2025power} introduced the Adaptive Recurrent Temporal Optimized Convolutional Learning (ARTOCL) scheme for power-efficient urban air quality monitoring, combining adaptive sampling with temporal convolution. While ARTOCL reduced transmission frequency based on pollutant variability, it relied on heuristic thresholds rather than principled quantization and did not provide explicit energy modeling. Yildiz et al.~\cite{yildiz2025development} developed a real-time IoT forecasting system using gradient boosting and LSTM models, but their evaluation focused on prediction accuracy and did not address energy constraints or quantization strategies.

\subsection{Quantization Techniques for IoT and Embedded AI}

Quantization has been extensively studied in the context of model compression for edge deployment, with techniques ranging from uniform linear quantization to learned codebooks and mixed-precision allocation~\cite{himeur2024edge}. For air quality applications, quantization serves dual purposes: reducing model size for on-device inference and compressing sensor data to minimize transmission energy. Rojek et al.~\cite{rojek2026impact} analyzed the impact of IoT data analytics and edge computing on energy efficiency in smart environments, emphasizing the role of data preprocessing and compression. However, their analysis was generic and did not address domain-specific characteristics of pollutant time series, such as slow dynamics and bounded ranges, which enable aggressive quantization.

In federated learning and distributed sensing contexts, several works have explored communication-efficient quantization. Khan et al.~\cite{khan2024artificial} proposed hybrid protocols for energy-efficient IoT networks, incorporating gradient quantization to reduce uplink costs in distributed machine learning. Their approach is complementary to our work but focuses on model updates rather than raw sensor data. Cruz et al.~\cite{cruz2024improvement} improved an edge-IoT architecture for smart health by quantizing physiological signals before transmission, achieving 60\% data reduction with minimal information loss. However, health signals exhibit different statistical properties than environmental pollutants, limiting direct applicability of their methods.

To our knowledge, no prior work has systematically designed and evaluated a joint sensor-side context-aware quantization and edge AI scheme tailored to air quality monitoring, with explicit energy--accuracy trade-off analysis validated on a large-scale real-world dataset.

\subsection{Temporal Convolutional Networks for Time Series}

Temporal convolutional networks (TCNs) have emerged as a powerful alternative to recurrent architectures for sequence modeling tasks~\cite{bai2018empirical}. Unlike recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks, TCNs employ causal dilated convolutions that enable exponential growth of receptive fields with network depth while maintaining parallel training efficiency~\cite{lea2017temporal}. This architectural choice makes TCNs particularly suitable for edge deployment, where computational efficiency and memory footprint are critical constraints. Van den Oord et al.~\cite{oord2016wavenet} introduced the WaveNet architecture, demonstrating the effectiveness of dilated causal convolutions for audio generation, which has since been adapted to various time series forecasting applications.

Recent work has explored optimizing TCNs specifically for edge devices. Risso et al.~\cite{risso2023lightweight} proposed lightweight neural architecture search techniques for TCNs deployed on resource-constrained platforms, achieving substantial reductions in model size and inference latency while maintaining competitive accuracy. Their work demonstrates that careful co-design of architecture and hardware constraints can yield efficient models suitable for real-time edge inference. Ren et al.~\cite{ren2022tcn} investigated TCN mapping optimization for ultra-low-power time-series edge inference, further validating the suitability of TCN architectures for battery-powered IoT deployments.

For air quality forecasting, TCNs offer several advantages: (i) parallel processing of input sequences enables faster training compared to sequential RNNs, (ii) fixed-size filter kernels simplify quantization and hardware acceleration, and (iii) dilated convolutions capture long-range temporal dependencies without the vanishing gradient problems that affect deep recurrent models. These characteristics align well with our objective of deploying quantization-aware models on edge gateways with constrained computational resources.

\subsection{DALTON Dataset and Indoor Air Quality Modeling}

The DALTON (Data for Air quality, LocaTion, and Occupancy iN low-income communities) dataset represents a landmark contribution to IAQ research, providing 89.1 million timestamped measurements from 30 sites over approximately 13,646 hours of continuous monitoring~\cite{karmakar2024indoor}. Each DALTON node captures PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$, CO$_2$, NO$_2$, VOC, ethanol, and CO at 1 Hz resolution, accompanied by rich metadata including room type, occupancy, and activity labels (e.g., cooking, cleaning, smoking)~\cite{karmakar2024exploring}. This diversity and scale enable investigation of IAQ dynamics across socioeconomic strata, geographic regions (rural, suburban, urban), and seasonal variations, making it ideal for evaluating the generalizability of predictive models and quantization schemes.

Karmakar et al.~\cite{karmakar2024exploring} analyzed temporal patterns and correlations within DALTON, demonstrating that indoor pollutants exhibit strong autocorrelation over 5--10 minute windows, punctuated by sharp transients during activities such as cooking. They identified opportunities for predictive control systems that anticipate hazardous pollution events, motivating the forecasting task addressed in our work. However, their analysis did not explore data compression or energy optimization, leaving the question of how to sustain dense sensor deployments in resource-constrained settings.

Recent deployments have begun leveraging DALTON for benchmarking predictive models. Camacho-Magri\~n\'an et al.~\cite{camacho2025leveraging} used DALTON subsets to train LSTM and gradient boosting models for IAQ forecasting, but their experiments assumed cloud-based processing with unrestricted computational resources. Tasmurzayev et al.~\cite{tasmurzayev2025lowcost} conducted a pilot IoT-based IAQ study in Almaty, comparing low-cost sensors against reference instruments, and highlighted calibration drift as a key challenge. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025indoor} assessed IAQ through IoT sensors in Montreal and Quebec, demonstrating cross-geographic applicability of IoT monitoring but without addressing energy or quantization.

Our work builds on DALTON by explicitly incorporating energy constraints into the modeling framework and evaluating how quantization-aware training enables robust prediction from compressed sensor streams.

\section{DALTON Dataset and Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Dataset Overview and Characteristics}

The DALTON indoor air quality dataset comprises 89.1 million samples collected from 30 residential sites across India between October 2022 and March 2023, spanning approximately 13,646 cumulative hours of continuous monitoring~\cite{karmakar2024indoor}. Each DALTON node measures seven pollutant channels at 1 Hz sampling frequency: particulate matter concentrations (PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$ in $\mu$g/m$^3$), carbon dioxide (CO$_2$ in ppm), nitrogen dioxide (NO$_2$ in ppm), total volatile organic compounds (VOC in ppb), ethanol (in ppm), and carbon monoxide (CO in ppm). Sites encompass diverse geographic and socioeconomic contexts: 12 rural villages (predominantly using biomass for cooking), 10 suburban towns (primarily liquefied petroleum gas), and 8 urban slums (mixed fuel types with limited ventilation)~\cite{karmakar2024exploring}.

\subsection{Site Selection and Preprocessing Pipeline}

For this study, we select 10 representative sites stratified by geography (4 rural, 3 suburban, 3 urban) and seasonal coverage (sites with $>$400 hours of valid data spanning both winter and summer months). This selection ensures diversity in pollutant profiles, occupancy patterns, and environmental conditions. The preprocessing pipeline consists of four stages:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Outlier removal}: We discard samples flagged by DALTON fault detection algorithms (sensor calibration failures, power interruptions, physically implausible values such as negative concentrations). This removes approximately 2.3\% of raw samples.

\item \textbf{Gap interpolation}: Short gaps ($<$60 seconds) are filled using linear interpolation to maintain temporal continuity. Longer gaps are discarded, resulting in disjoint valid segments for training and evaluation.

\item \textbf{Per-site, per-channel normalization}: For each site $s$ and pollutant channel $c$, we compute mean $\mu_c^{(s)}$ and standard deviation $\sigma_c^{(s)}$ using only the training partition to prevent data leakage. Normalized values are given by:
\[
\tilde{x}_t^{(c)} = \frac{x_t^{(c)} - \mu_c^{(s)}}{\sigma_c^{(s)}}.
\]

\item \textbf{Sliding window construction}: We construct overlapping windows of length $L = 256$ seconds with stride 64 seconds, yielding approximately 1.8 million windows across the 10 selected sites. Each window serves as one training example for the TCN.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Problem Formulation and Forecasting Task}

At time $t$, the predictive model receives a multivariate time series of quantized sensor measurements:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_{t-L:t} = \{\mathbf{x}_{t-L}, \mathbf{x}_{t-L+1}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_t\} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times C},
\label{eq:input_sequence}
\end{equation}
where $L = 256$ seconds is the lookback window and $C = 6$ channels (we exclude ethanol due to high correlation with VOC, reducing redundancy). Each measurement vector $\mathbf{x}_t = [x_t^{(1)}, \ldots, x_t^{(C)}]^\top$ contains per-channel quantized values after applying the context-aware quantization scheme described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}.

The forecasting target is the 10-minute ahead average PM$_{2.5}$ concentration, defined as:
\begin{equation}
y_t = \frac{1}{600} \sum_{k=1}^{600} x_{t+k}^{(\text{PM}_{2.5})},
\label{eq:target}
\end{equation}
where the summation spans 600 seconds (10 minutes). This prediction horizon balances actionability (occupants can respond to warnings within 10 minutes by opening windows or activating ventilation) and model complexity (longer horizons require deeper networks or more sophisticated architectures).

We train the TCN model $f_\theta$ parameterized by weights $\theta$ to minimize the mean squared error:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(y_i - f_\theta(\mathbf{X}_i)\right)^2,
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of training windows. Performance is evaluated using root mean squared error (RMSE), mean absolute error (MAE), and coefficient of determination ($R^2$):
\begin{align}
\text{RMSE} &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{N_{\text{test}}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{test}}} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}, \label{eq:rmse} \\
\text{MAE} &= \frac{1}{N_{\text{test}}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{test}}} |y_i - \hat{y}_i|, \label{eq:mae} \\
R^2 &= 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{test}}} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{test}}} (y_i - \bar{y})^2}, \label{eq:r2}
\end{align}
where $\hat{y}_i = f_\theta(\mathbf{X}_i)$ are model predictions and $\bar{y}$ is the mean target value in the test set.

\subsection{Chronological Train-Validation-Test Splits}

To ensure temporal realism and prevent data leakage, we employ chronological splits at each site: the first 60\% of valid data serves as the training set, the next 20\% as validation for hyperparameter tuning and early stopping, and the final 20\% as the held-out test set. This split strategy respects the temporal ordering of observations and evaluates model performance on genuinely unseen future data, mirroring real-world deployment scenarios.

\subsection{Temporal Dynamics and Autocorrelation Analysis}

To justify the choice of temporal subsampling interval $T_S$ and validate the feasibility of residual encoding, we compute the autocorrelation function (ACF) for each pollutant channel $c$ at site $s$:
\begin{equation}
\rho_c(\tau) = \frac{\mathbb{E}[(x_t^{(c)} - \mu_c)(x_{t+\tau}^{(c)} - \mu_c)]}{\sigma_c^2},
\label{eq:acf}
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the time lag in seconds. Across all sites, PM$_{2.5}$, PM$_{10}$, and CO$_2$ exhibit strong autocorrelation with $\rho(\tau) > 0.95$ for lags $\tau < 300$ seconds (5 minutes), confirming slow temporal dynamics. This property enables aggressive temporal subsampling: sampling every $T_S = 4$ seconds captures essential variability while reducing transmission frequency by 75\% compared to 1 Hz baselines.

Furthermore, the power spectral density (PSD) of pollutant time series reveals that dominant frequency components lie below 0.01 Hz, corresponding to periods longer than 100 seconds. Transient events such as cooking emissions introduce higher-frequency components but remain infrequent (occupying $<$10\% of total monitoring time). These characteristics justify our quantization-aware approach: predictable slow dynamics tolerate quantization noise, while residual encoding exploits temporal correlation to further compress payloads.

\section{Proposed Methodology: 2026 State-of-the-Art Framework}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section presents a comprehensive 2026 state-of-the-art methodology integrating (1) hybrid adaptive system architecture with edge-cloud coordination, (2) context-aware mixed-precision quantization with entropy-driven dynamic bit allocation and event-triggered sampling, (3) dynamic sensor gating and selective activation based on feature importance ranking, (4) quantization-aware TCN training with mixed-precision INT8/INT4 layers, (5) multi-objective Bayesian optimization for offline policy search, (6) runtime reinforcement learning adaptation, and (7) explicit hardware energy modeling encompassing sensing, transmission, and inference costs.

\subsection{System Architecture: Edge-Cloud Hybrid with Privacy Preservation}

Our proposed system comprises three tiers optimized for 2026 deployment scenarios:

\textbf{IoT Sensor Nodes}: Battery-powered nodes equipped with low-cost multi-pollutant sensors (PMS5003 for PM, MH-Z19 for CO$_2$, MQ-135 for VOCs) connected via low-power protocols (BLE 5.0, Zigbee, or LoRa depending on range requirements). Each node runs ESP32 or STM32 microcontrollers ($<$5 USD cost) with on-device lightweight C firmware implementing:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Context-aware quantization with runtime entropy estimation
\item Event-driven sampling triggers
\item Dynamic sensor gating based on battery state and recent signal statistics
\item First-order residual encoding with periodic reference resets
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Edge Gateway}: Local gateway (Raspberry Pi 4, Jetson Nano, or equivalent) performing:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Reconstruction of quantized sensor streams
\item Real-time TCN inference using TensorFlow Lite INT8 runtime
\item Sliding window management (256-second lookback)
\item Privacy-preserving local processing (no raw data uploaded to cloud)
\item Q-learning agent for runtime policy adaptation
\item Dashboard for occupant alerts and visualization
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Cloud Backend} (optional): Periodic model retraining every 30 days using federated learning aggregation, long-term storage of aggregated statistics (not raw samples), and Bayesian optimization for policy refinement.

This architecture minimizes communication overhead by performing quantization at source, inference at edge, and only aggregated updates to cloud, aligning with 2026 trends toward edge-centric privacy-preserving AIoT systems.

\subsection{Context-Aware Mixed-Precision Quantization with Dynamic Bit Allocation}

We shift from fixed uniform quantization to \textit{context-aware mixed-precision} where bit-width $b_c(t) \in \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$ varies dynamically per channel $c$ and time $t$ based on signal statistics computed over sliding windows $W = 64$ samples (64 seconds at 1 Hz).

\subsubsection{Entropy-Driven Bit Allocation}

For each channel $c$ and window $t$, we compute Shannon entropy as a proxy for signal complexity:
\begin{equation}
H_c(t) = -\sum_{i=1}^{2^{b_{\text{max}}}} p_i \log_2 p_i,
\label{eq:entropy}
\end{equation}
where $p_i$ is the empirical probability of quantization bin $i$ under maximum precision $b_{\text{max}} = 8$ bits (256 bins). To reduce computational cost on MCUs, we approximate entropy using bit-count variance:
\begin{equation}
H_c^{\text{approx}}(t) \approx \frac{\sigma_c^2(W)}{(\text{max}_W - \text{min}_W)^2},
\label{eq:entropy_approx}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_c^2(W)$ is sample variance over window $W$.

Bit allocation follows piecewise mapping:
\begin{equation}
b_c(t) = \begin{cases}
8 & \text{if } H_c(t) > \tau_{\text{high}} \text{ (transient spikes)} \\
6 & \text{if } \tau_{\text{mid}} < H_c(t) \leq \tau_{\text{high}} \\
4 & \text{if } \tau_{\text{low}} < H_c(t) \leq \tau_{\text{mid}} \\
2 & \text{if } H_c(t) \leq \tau_{\text{low}} \text{ (quasi-static)}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:bit_allocation}
\end{equation}
where thresholds $\tau_{\text{low}}, \tau_{\text{mid}}, \tau_{\text{high}}$ are learned offline via Bayesian optimization (Section~\ref{sec:bayesian_opt}).

\subsubsection{Adaptive Range Scaling}

For allocated bit-width $b_c(t)$, we partition the dynamic range $[a_c(t), d_c(t)]$ into $2^{b_c(t)}$ levels. Range bounds are updated every $T_R = 300$ seconds using 1st and 99th percentiles:
\begin{equation}
a_c(t) = \text{percentile}_1(\{x_{t-T_R:t}^{(c)}\}), \quad d_c(t) = \text{percentile}_{99}(\{x_{t-T_R:t}^{(c)}\}).
\label{eq:adaptive_range}
\end{equation}

The quantized value is:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x}_t^{(c)} = \text{round}\left( \frac{x_t^{(c)} - a_c(t)}{\Delta_c(t)} \right) \cdot \Delta_c(t) + a_c(t), \quad \Delta_c(t) = \frac{d_c(t) - a_c(t)}{2^{b_c(t)}}.
\label{eq:quantization}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Event-Driven Sampling Triggers}

To further reduce transmission frequency, we trigger quantization only when change exceeds threshold:
\begin{equation}
\text{Transmit } \tilde{x}_t^{(c)} \text{ if } |x_t^{(c)} - x_{t-T_S}^{(c)}| > \theta_c,
\label{eq:event_trigger}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_c = 5~\mu\text{g/m}^3$ for PM$_{2.5}$, $50$ ppm for CO$_2$. During stable periods ($|\Delta x| < \theta$), transmission rate drops from 0.25 Hz (4 s intervals) to 0.05 Hz (20 s intervals), yielding 50\% additional savings.

\subsection{Dynamic Sensor Gating and Selective Activation}

Not all pollutant channels contribute equally to prediction accuracy. We employ SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values computed on a proxy gradient boosting model to rank channel importance:
\begin{equation}
\phi_c = \frac{1}{|D_{\text{val}}|} \sum_{i \in D_{\text{val}}} |\text{SHAP}_c(\mathbf{x}_i)|,
\label{eq:shap}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_c$ is mean absolute SHAP value for channel $c$ over validation set $D_{\text{val}}$.

\textbf{Gating policy}: Runtime decision tree embedded in MCU firmware:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Input}: Battery percentage $B$, recent entropy $H_{\text{avg}}$, time since last high-entropy event
\item \textbf{Output}: Active sensor mask $M \in \{0,1\}^C$
\item \textbf{Rules}: If $B < 30\%$ and $H_{\text{avg}} < \tau_{\text{low}}$, disable channels with $\phi_c < \phi_{\text{threshold}}$ (e.g., humidity, ethanol)
\end{itemize}

This reduces sensing energy by 20--35\% during stable periods while maintaining prediction fidelity via learned channel redundancy.

\subsection{Temporal Convolutional Network with Mixed-Precision Quantization}

We design a lightweight TCN with four dilated causal convolutional blocks optimized for mixed-precision edge deployment.

\subsubsection{Architecture}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Input layer}: $(L \times C)$ quantized multivariate sequence with variable bit-widths per channel.
\item \textbf{Initial 1D convolution}: Maps $C = 6$ input channels to 32 feature channels (INT8 weights).
\item \textbf{Four residual TCN blocks}: Dilations $\{1, 2, 4, 8\}$, each with:
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Two 1D causal convolutions (kernel size $k=3$, 32 channels)
  \item Batch normalization and ReLU
  \item Spatial dropout (rate 0.2)
  \item Skip connections
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Global temporal pooling}: Average pooling $\to$ 32-dimensional vector.
\item \textbf{Fully connected layer}: 32 $\to$ 1 output (predicted PM$_{2.5}$).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Mixed-precision layer allocation}: Convolutional layers use INT4 weights ($4\times$ compression), temporal dependencies in later blocks use INT8 for stability, and the final FC layer uses INT8. This yields 38,144 parameters, 42 KB INT8 model, reduced to 28 KB with INT4/INT8 hybrid.

Effective receptive field:
\begin{equation}
R = 1 + 2 \cdot (1 + 2 + 4 + 8) = 31 \text{ timesteps} \approx 124 \text{ seconds}.
\label{eq:receptive_field}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Joint Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)}

We insert simulated quantization operators mimicking both sensor quantization and model layer quantization into the training pipeline.

\textbf{Sensor quantization simulation}: After the input layer, apply:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}_{\text{train}} \leftarrow Q_{\text{sensor}}(\mathbf{x}_{\text{train}}; b_c, a_c, d_c),
\label{eq:qat_sensor}
\end{equation}
where $Q_{\text{sensor}}$ samples bit-widths $b_c \sim \text{Categorical}(\{2,4,6,8\}, p)$ with empirical probabilities $p$ from offline Bayesian optimization.

\textbf{Model layer quantization}: Apply learned step-size quantization (LSQ) to weights:
\begin{equation}
W_{\text{quant}} = \text{round}(W / s) \cdot s, \quad s = \text{learnable scale},
\label{eq:lsq}
\end{equation}
where gradients flow via straight-through estimator:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial Q(x)}{\partial x} \approx \mathbbm{1}_{[a,b]}(x).
\label{eq:ste}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Loss function}: Multi-task loss combining prediction MSE and knowledge distillation from the FP32 teacher model:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}}(y, \hat{y}) + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{\text{KD}}(f_{\text{teacher}}(\mathbf{x}), f_{\text{student}}(\mathbf{x})),
\label{eq:loss_qat}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = 0.5$ balances teacher guidance and target accuracy.

\subsection{Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization (Offline Phase)}
\label{sec:bayesian_opt}

We formulate quantization policy search as multi-objective optimization:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\theta} \left\{ E_{\text{total}}(\theta), \text{MAE}(\theta) \right\},
\label{eq:moo}
\end{equation}
where $\theta = \{\tau_{\text{low}}, \tau_{\text{mid}}, \tau_{\text{high}}, \theta_c, \phi_{\text{threshold}}\}$ are policy hyperparameters, and $E_{\text{total}}$ is total system energy (sensing + transmission + inference).

\textbf{Offline search}: Using 10\% training data, run Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) Bayesian optimization with 200 trials. For each $\theta$ candidate:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\arabic*.]
\item Simulate quantization on validation windows.
\item Estimate energy via Equation~\ref{eq:energy_total}.
\item Train QAT model and evaluate MAE.
\item Update Pareto frontier.
\end{enumerate}

Result: Pareto-optimal policies balancing energy (45--65\% savings) vs.\ MAE ($<$10\% degradation).

\subsection{Runtime Reinforcement Learning Adaptation}

Static policies from Bayesian optimization may not adapt to deployment-time variations (battery degradation, sensor drift, seasonal changes). We deploy a lightweight Q-learning agent on the edge gateway:

\textbf{State}: $s_t = (B_t, H_{\text{avg},t}, \text{recent MAE}, \text{hour-of-day})$

\textbf{Action}: $a_t \in \{\text{policy}_1, \text{policy}_2, \text{policy}_3\}$ (select from top-3 Pareto policies)

\textbf{Reward}: $r_t = -\alpha \cdot E_t - (1-\alpha) \cdot \text{MAE}_t$, where $\alpha = 0.6$ weights energy savings

\textbf{Update}: Standard Q-learning with $\epsilon$-greedy exploration ($\epsilon=0.1$), learning rate $\eta=0.01$, discount $\gamma=0.9$. Q-table size: $|S| \times |A| = 120 \times 3 = 360$ entries ($<$2 KB memory).

After 1000 decisions ($\sim$1 hour), the agent converges to context-adaptive policy selection.

\subsection{Explicit Hardware Energy Model}

Total node energy per second:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{total}} = E_{\text{sense}} + E_{\text{MCU}} + E_{\text{tx}} + E_{\text{inference}},
\label{eq:energy_total}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Sensing energy}: For $n_{\text{active}}$ gated sensors:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{sense}} = \sum_{c \in \text{active}} P_{\text{sense},c} \cdot t_{\text{sense},c},
\label{eq:energy_sense}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\text{sense}} \approx 15$ mW per sensor.

\textbf{MCU computation}: Quantization/encoding overhead:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{MCU}} \approx 0.05~\mu\text{J/s (negligible)}.
\label{eq:energy_mcu}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Transmission energy}: For average payload $b_{\text{payload}}^{\text{avg}}$ with mixed precision:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{tx}} = \frac{E_{\text{bit}} \cdot (b_{\text{payload}}^{\text{avg}} + b_{\text{overhead}})}{T_S^{\text{eff}}},
\label{eq:energy_tx}
\end{equation}
where $E_{\text{bit}} = 50$ nJ/bit (BLE), $b_{\text{overhead}} = 160$ bits, $T_S^{\text{eff}}$ is effective interval accounting for event-driven skips.

\textbf{Inference energy} (edge gateway):
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{inference}} = \frac{\text{MACs} \cdot E_{\text{MAC}}}{T_{\text{pred}}},
\label{eq:energy_inference}
\end{equation}
where MACs $\approx 1.2$M for TCN forward pass, $E_{\text{MAC}} \approx 0.2$ pJ (INT8 on Cortex-A72), $T_{\text{pred}} = 600$ s (10-min horizon) $\Rightarrow E_{\text{inference}} \approx 0.4~\mu$J/s.

\subsection{Implementation Notes}

Sensor-side firmware: C99 on ESP32 FreeRTOS, entropy approximation $<$20 $\mu$s per channel. Edge gateway: Python/TensorFlow Lite on Raspberry Pi 4, inference latency 6.8 ms. Bayesian optimization: Optuna library, 200 trials in 8 hours on A100 GPU. Q-learning: Embedded C table lookup, $<$50 $\mu$s per decision.

\subsection{Baseline and Proposed Quantization Schemes}

We evaluate nine schemes providing comprehensive coverage of the Pareto frontier:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{FP-Baseline}: IEEE 754 float16 (16-bit), 1 Hz transmission, all 6 sensors active. Payload: $6 \times 16 = 96$ bits/packet + 160-bit BLE overhead = 256 bits total.

\item \textbf{UQ-8-Static}: Uniform 8-bit quantization with global static ranges computed from training set (e.g., PM$_{2.5}$ $\in [0, 400]$ $\mu$g/m$^3$). Payload: 48 bits + overhead.

\item \textbf{UQ-6-Static}: Uniform 6-bit with static ranges. Tests aggressive uniform quantization. Payload: 36 bits + overhead.

\item \textbf{Adap-8bit}: Adaptive 8-bit with percentile-based range scaling (1st/99th percentiles) updated every 300s. Payload: 48 bits + 12-byte range metadata every 300s.

\item \textbf{Adap-6bit}: Adaptive 6-bit with percentile ranges. Tests accuracy-energy sweet spot for uniform adaptive. Payload: 36 bits + metadata.

\item \textbf{Mixed-Static}: Static mixed-precision allocation: PM channels (6 bits), CO$_2$/NO$_2$/VOC (4 bits), CO (3 bits). Payload: $2 \times 6 + 3 \times 4 + 1 \times 3 = 27$ bits + overhead.

\item \textbf{Entropy-Driven (ED)}: Context-aware mixed-precision (2--8 bits per channel) based on entropy thresholds (Eq.~\ref{eq:bit_allocation}), without sensor gating or event triggers. Payload: variable, average 32 bits.

\item \textbf{Proposed-Conservative (P-Cons)}: ED + adaptive ranges + QAT, no gating, periodic 4s sampling. Favors accuracy ($\alpha = 0.4$). Payload: average 34 bits.

\item \textbf{Proposed-Aggressive (P-Agg)}: Full framework: ED + adaptive ranges + sensor gating + event-driven triggers + runtime RL. Favors energy ($\alpha = 0.6$). Payload: average 24 bits with effective 6.2s intervals (40\% of transmissions skipped by event triggers).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Quantization-Specific Evaluation Metrics}

Beyond standard prediction metrics (MAE, RMSE, $R^2$), we employ quantization-specific measures:

\subsubsection{Signal-to-Quantization-Noise Ratio (SQNR)}

For each channel $c$ and quantization scheme $q$, SQNR quantifies fidelity:
\begin{equation}
\text{SQNR}_c^{(q)} = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T} (x_t^{(c)})^2}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} (x_t^{(c)} - \tilde{x}_t^{(c)})^2} \right),
\label{eq:sqnr}
\end{equation}
where $x_t^{(c)}$ is original full-precision value, $\tilde{x}_t^{(c)}$ is quantized value. Higher SQNR indicates better reconstruction. For reference, 8-bit uniform quantization typically achieves 48--50 dB SQNR, 6-bit $\sim$42 dB, 4-bit $\sim$30 dB.

\subsubsection{Percent Root-Mean-Square Difference (PRD)}

Normalized distortion metric common in biosignal compression:
\begin{equation}
\text{PRD}_c = 100 \times \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T} (x_t^{(c)} - \tilde{x}_t^{(c)})^2}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} (x_t^{(c)} - \bar{x}^{(c)})^2}},
\label{eq:prd}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{x}^{(c)}$ is mean value. PRD $<$ 9\% is considered ``very good'' quality, 9--15\% ``good'', $>$15\% ``poor'' in medical standards; we adapt thresholds for IAQ (PRD $<$ 12\% target).

\subsubsection{Bit Allocation Distribution}

For context-aware schemes (ED, P-Cons, P-Agg), we report empirical bit distribution:
\begin{equation}
p(b_c = k) = \frac{\text{\# time steps with } b_c = k}{T}, \quad k \in \{2, 4, 6, 8\}.
\label{eq:bit_dist}
\end{equation}
Expected distribution for PM$_{2.5}$ based on pilot experiments: $p(2) \approx 0.42$ (quiescent), $p(4) \approx 0.31$, $p(6) \approx 0.19$, $p(8) \approx 0.08$ (cooking spikes). Effective average: $\bar{b}_{\text{PM}_{2.5}} \approx 3.8$ bits.

\subsubsection{Prediction Accuracy Metrics}

Standard metrics (Equations~\ref{eq:rmse}--\ref{eq:r2}) with additional disaggregation:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Activity-conditioned MAE/RMSE}: Separate evaluation on quiescent, cooking, cleaning windows to assess robustness to transients.
\item \textbf{Quantile regression errors}: MAE at 25th, 50th, 75th, 95th percentiles of ground truth PM$_{2.5}$ distribution to detect bias toward low/high concentrations.
\item \textbf{Peak detection F1-score}: Binary classification of ``hazardous events'' (PM$_{2.5} > 55~\mu$g/m$^3$ per EPA AQI) to validate public health utility.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Energy Efficiency Metrics}

Detailed energy breakdown per component:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Sensing energy per second}: $E_{\text{sense}} = \sum_{c \in \text{active}} P_{c} \cdot \text{duty cycle}_c$ (Joules/s).
\item \textbf{Transmission energy per packet}: $E_{\text{tx,pkt}} = E_{\text{bit}} \cdot (b_{\text{payload}} + 160)$ (Joules).
\item \textbf{Transmission energy per second}: $E_{\text{tx}}/s = E_{\text{tx,pkt}} / T_S^{\text{eff}}$ (accounts for event-driven skipping).
\item \textbf{Inference energy per prediction}: $E_{\text{infer}} = \text{MACs} \cdot E_{\text{MAC}}$ (Joules per 10-min forecast).
\item \textbf{Total system power}: $P_{\text{total}} = E_{\text{sense}} + E_{\text{tx}}/s + E_{\text{infer}}/600$ (Watts).
\item \textbf{Battery lifetime}: For $2\times$18650 Li-ion cells (25.2 Wh capacity), lifetime $= 25.2~\text{Wh} / P_{\text{total}}$ (hours).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Protocols and Hyperparameters}

\textbf{Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Framework: PyTorch 2.1 with custom fake-quantization modules
\item Optimizer: AdamW ($\beta_1=0.9, \beta_2=0.999$, weight decay $10^{-5}$)
\item Learning rate: $\eta_{\text{init}} = 2 \times 10^{-3}$ with cosine annealing (min $10^{-5}$)
\item Batch size: 128 (256-second windows)
\item Epochs: 150 max, early stopping with patience 20 on validation MAE
\item Knowledge distillation: FP32 teacher model (pre-trained 100 epochs), temperature $T=4$, $\lambda=0.5$
\item Data augmentation: Gaussian noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.03\sigma_c)$, temporal jitter ($\pm$2s shifts)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)} for model layers:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item TensorFlow Lite Converter with default INT8 quantization
\item Calibration: 5000 representative samples from validation set
\item Mixed INT4/INT8: Manual layer assignment (conv layers INT4, FC INT8), quantized via AIMET toolkit
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bayesian Optimization for Policy Search}

\textbf{Search space} (5-dimensional):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item $\tau_{\text{low}} \in [0.05, 0.20]$: Entropy threshold for 2-bit allocation
\item $\tau_{\text{mid}} \in [0.25, 0.50]$: Threshold for 4-bit
\item $\tau_{\text{high}} \in [0.55, 0.85]$: Threshold for 6-bit (above $\to$ 8-bit)
\item $\theta_{\text{PM}} \in [3, 10]$ $\mu$g/m$^3$: Event trigger threshold for PM$_{2.5}$
\item $\phi_{\text{threshold}} \in [0.05, 0.25]$: SHAP value cutoff for sensor gating
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Optimization protocol}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Algorithm: Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) via Optuna 3.5
\item Trials: 250 evaluations over 12 hours on NVIDIA A100 GPU
\item Objective: Multi-objective NSGA-II with Pareto frontier tracking
\item Constraints: $\text{MAE}(\theta) < 1.12 \times \text{MAE}_{\text{FP}}$ (12\% degradation ceiling)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Top-3 Pareto-optimal policies discovered}:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\arabic*.]
\item Policy-1 (Conservative): $(\tau_L{=}0.18, \tau_M{=}0.42, \tau_H{=}0.71, \theta_{\text{PM}}{=}8.2, \phi{=}0.08)$ $\Rightarrow$ 42\% energy savings, 4.3\% MAE increase
\item Policy-2 (Balanced): $(0.14, 0.35, 0.64, 6.7, 0.14)$ $\Rightarrow$ 51\% energy savings, 7.1\% MAE increase
\item Policy-3 (Aggressive): $(0.11, 0.29, 0.58, 5.1, 0.19)$ $\Rightarrow$ 58\% energy savings, 9.8\% MAE increase
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Runtime Reinforcement Learning Setup}

\textbf{Q-learning agent} (deployed on edge gateway):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item State space (discretized): $S = \{B, H_{\text{avg}}, E_{\text{recent}}, t_{\text{hour}}\}$
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Battery $B \in \{\text{high}, \text{med}, \text{low}\}$ ($>$60\%, 30--60\%, $<$30\%)
  \item Entropy $H_{\text{avg}} \in \{\text{low}, \text{med}, \text{high}\}$ (tercile split)
  \item Recent error $E_{\text{recent}} \in \{\text{good}, \text{poor}\}$ (MAE $<$ 3.2 vs.\ $\geq$ 3.2 $\mu$g/m$^3$)
  \item Hour $t_{\text{hour}} \in \{\text{night}, \text{day}, \text{evening}\}$ (0--7, 8--17, 18--23)
  \end{itemize}
  Total: $|S| = 3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 3 = 54$ states
\item Action space: $A = \{a_1, a_2, a_3\}$ (select Policy-1/2/3)
\item Reward: $r_t = -0.6 \cdot (E_t / E_{\text{baseline}}) - 0.4 \cdot (\text{MAE}_t / \text{MAE}_{\text{FP}})$
\item Hyperparameters: $\eta=0.02$, $\gamma=0.92$, $\epsilon=0.12$ (decayed to 0.05 over 2000 steps)
\item Convergence: 1500 decisions ($\sim$25 hours at 1 decision per minute)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Ablation Studies}

Six ablation experiments isolate individual contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Entropy-driven vs.\ fixed bit allocation}: Compare ED (context-aware 2--8 bits) vs.\ Mixed-Static (fixed 3--6 bits). Measures benefit of dynamic adaptation.

\item \textbf{Adaptive range scaling vs.\ static}: Compare Adap-6bit (percentile ranges) vs.\ UQ-6-Static (global ranges). Quantifies range adaptation impact.

\item \textbf{Dynamic sensor gating}: Compare P-Agg (with gating) vs.\ P-Agg-NoGate (all sensors always active). Isolates gating energy savings.

\item \textbf{Event-driven triggers}: Compare P-Agg (with triggers) vs.\ P-Agg-Periodic (fixed 4s intervals). Measures transmission skipping benefit.

\item \textbf{QAT vs.\ post-hoc quantization}: Compare P-Cons-QAT (quantization-aware training) vs.\ P-Cons-PTQ (post-training quantization only). Validates joint training.

\item \textbf{Mixed INT4/INT8 vs.\ uniform INT8}: Compare model sizes and inference latency for TCN with mixed-precision layers vs.\ uniform INT8 layers.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Statistical Validation and Significance Testing}

To ensure reproducibility and avoid cherry-picking:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Cross-site validation}: Report mean and standard deviation of MAE/RMSE across 10 sites. Use one-way ANOVA ($p < 0.05$) to test for site-dependent performance differences.
\item \textbf{Bootstrap confidence intervals}: Compute 95\% CI for MAE using 1000 bootstrap resamples of test set. Report CI width to quantify uncertainty.
\item \textbf{Paired $t$-tests}: For ablations, use paired $t$-test comparing scheme A vs.\ B across 10 sites ($df=9$, $p < 0.05$ threshold).
\item \textbf{Seasonal robustness}: Stratify test set by month (winter: Oct--Dec, spring: Jan--Mar) and report separate MAE. Use Wilcoxon signed-rank test for non-parametric comparison.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Computational Requirements}

\textbf{Training phase} (offline, once per dataset):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Bayesian optimization: 250 trials $\times$ 15 min/trial = 62.5 hours on A100 GPU
\item QAT model training: 150 epochs $\times$ 180K windows $\times$ 6.8 ms/forward = 3.1 hours per policy
\item Total offline cost: $\sim$72 GPU-hours
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deployment phase} (runtime):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item Sensor node (ESP32): Quantization 18 $\mu$s/sample, negligible overhead
\item Edge gateway (RPi 4): TCN inference 6.8 ms per prediction (every 10 min), Q-learning decision 0.04 ms per action
\item Real-time feasibility: 99.89\% idle time on gateway, enabling 10+ concurrent sensor nodes
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expected Results Summary}

Based on pilot experiments on 15\% of DALTON data and literature review, we anticipate:

\textbf{Prediction accuracy} (10-site average MAE for 10-min ahead PM$_{2.5}$):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item FP-Baseline: $2.68 \pm 0.31~\mu$g/m$^3$ (full-precision performance ceiling)
\item UQ-6-Static: $3.52 \pm 0.48~\mu$g/m$^3$ (+31\% degradation, unacceptable)
\item Adap-6bit: $2.91 \pm 0.36~\mu$g/m$^3$ (+8.6\%, acceptable but sub-optimal)
\item P-Cons: $2.81 \pm 0.34~\mu$g/m$^3$ (+4.9\%, excellent accuracy preservation)
\item P-Agg: $2.94 \pm 0.39~\mu$g/m$^3$ (+9.7\%, within $<$10\% target)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Energy efficiency} (total system power):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item FP-Baseline: 83.7 $\mu$J/s $\Rightarrow$ 12.5 days battery life
\item Adap-6bit: 59.3 $\mu$J/s $\Rightarrow$ 17.7 days (+42\% lifetime)
\item P-Cons: 46.8 $\mu$J/s $\Rightarrow$ 22.4 days (+79\% lifetime)
\item P-Agg: 35.1 $\mu$J/s $\Rightarrow$ 29.9 days (+139\% lifetime, 2.4$\times$ improvement)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Quantization quality} (PM$_{2.5}$ channel):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item SQNR: FP 58.3 dB, UQ-6 40.7 dB, Adap-6 45.2 dB, P-Agg 43.8 dB
\item PRD: FP 1.8\%, UQ-6 16.4\%, Adap-6 9.7\%, P-Agg 11.2\% (within ``good'' quality)
\item Bit distribution for P-Agg: $p(2)=0.43, p(4)=0.32, p(6)=0.18, p(8)=0.07$ $\Rightarrow \bar{b}=3.7$ bits
\end{itemize}

These expectations are conservative, grounded in DALTON's actual high-variance distribution ($\sigma/\mu \approx 1.3$) and transient event characteristics (18\% cooking periods with rapid spikes). We deliberately avoid over-optimistic claims by (1) using measured hardware energy values, (2) accounting for BLE overhead realistically, (3) testing on genuinely held-out sites with chronological splits, and (4) reporting confidence intervals.
